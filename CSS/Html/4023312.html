1. Glossary of terms 

  • Resolution: Presented as a number x another number (for example, 4096 x 2304). This is the number of pixels displayed on the screen.

  • Hertz (Hz): A number representing the refresh rate of a monitor or display. This represents the number of times per second that the display is updated. The higher the Hz value, the better quality the picture. Surface Dock (with Surface Connect) supports 30Hz and 60Hz.

  • Display Port: A type of display output and connector that is used by Surface Dock (with Surface Connect). The Surface Dock’s Display Port 1.2 chipset is 17.28 Gbit/s. This does not mean 17.28Gbit/s per Display Port but instead total throughput of 17.28Gbit/s through the Dock.

  • Surface Connect: The connector that connects the Surface to the Surface Dock. Surface Connect plugs into the Surface where the Surface PSU connects as well.

  • Adapter: Used to convert signal from one type of connector to another. For example, a MiniDP to HDMI adapter has a MiniDP connector on one side and HDMI adapter on the other side. See this list of recommended adapters.

  • Nvidia GPU, dGPU, DGPU, discrete: These are terms used to describe the Nvidia display adapter in products such as Surface Book.

  • DisplayLink: Technology used to connect additional displays (and other components) through a USB connection. It is not supported by Surface. For more information see the third-party DisplayLink site.

2. Information you need to know 

  • Intel graphics (the chipset that Surface uses) only allows for a maximum of three displays at one time. This includes the built-in Surface Display.

  • The public article Connect Surface to a TV, monitor, or projector outlines how many monitors and what resolutions (Hz) are supported.

  • Surface Dock splits the total available bandwidth as described in DisplayPort 1.2 (17.28 Gbit/s) specification between the two mini Display ports (mDP). For example, two 4k monitors will only work at 30Hz if both utilize the Dock’s mini DisplayPort outputs.

3. Network problems

Confirming the issue

When an ethernet cable is plugged into the Surface Dock and the customer says:

  • The ethernet icon does not appear on the taskbar.

  • The ethernet icon shows as not connected (red x) or limited connection (yellow exclamation point).

  • They can’t connect to Wi-Fi while docked but can connect while undocked.

[Asset 4465445]

Confirmation/paraphrase

“Just to make sure I understand your issue: When an ethernet cable is plugged into the Surface Dock, the connection is not working as expected while the device is docked, correct?”

Do the following

  2. Disconnect all USB peripherals. Leave the MiniDP connections for external monitors.
  3. Unplug the power cord. Note: Unplug the power cord from power brick. Do not just unplug power from back power port of Dock. Check if it is plugged into a UPS or power strip. If so, try plugging the Surface Dock directly into power.
 This helps to determine if reseating connections and power will help resolve issue.

Document the error/notification message if unable to load bing.com.

Be sure to go to the same website, using the same browser to reproduce. Use Edge instead of third-party browsers to initially troubleshoot and isolate the issue.

7. Run the Networking Troubleshooter.

Go to Settings > Update & Security > Troubleshoot > Network Adapter.

Select Run the troubleshooter.

 

The troubleshooter may detect an issue that you are unable to resolve. In these instances, you should work to determine whether the Dock’s Ethernet port or the Surface’s network card is functioning.

Turn off/unplug the modem and router or Gateway (modem and router combined).

Wait for 30 seconds, and then plug-in and turn it back on again.

Power cycling the modem and router or gateway clears the networking cache and allows for better performance and may improve the customer’s network speeds.

Note: 

If the customer is using chat or VOIP this step may disconnect from the call. Get the necessary information to call them back.

Go to Start > Power > Restart.

This will refresh drivers, and memory cache; as well as re-establish an internet connection.

If the customer has an alternate ethernet cable, they should try that cable. This will help eliminate whether the ethernet cable is the cause.

It may be in the customer’s best interest to attempt an alternate cable. If the cable is the issue, then replacing the Dock will not resolve the problem, and they will still have to replace the cable.

16. Connect directly to the Surface by using a USB to ethernet adapter.

If the ethernet connection works while connected directly to the Surface via a USB to ethernet adapter, the issue is with the Dock. Replace the Dock.

Making sure Surface and Windows are up to date may help avoid this problem in the future.

No, replace the Dock: Set up an exchange for the customer based on the status of their warranty.

Use KB 2791320 Informational: Surface: Exchange and fulfillment landing page

There is likely a hardware problem with the Surface Dock.

4. Audio problems

While docked, the Surface doesn’t play audio through an external device that is also connected to the Dock through any of the following:

  • The 3.5-mm/headphone style connection in the back of the Dock

  • A standard USB connection to the Dock

  • A Bluetooth dongle connected to the Dock’s USB ports

 The following symptoms may also occur:

[Asset 4465446] The sound icon is displayed, but no audio plays through the Surface speakers or connected external speakers.
[Asset 4465447] The sound icon appears as muted (there’s an "x" next to the icon).
[Asset 4465448] No sound icon is displayed on the taskbar.

7. Force the device to shut down and restart.

(Process varies by new vs. old generation.)

Studio (all), Laptop (all), Pro (5th Gen), Pro 6, Book 2, and Go: Press and hold the power button for 20 seconds.

Other devices: Press and hold the volume-up button and the power button at the same time for at least 15 seconds and then release both.

The Surface may boot to UEFI. This is normal. Have the customer exit UEFI.

A video is available at Force a shut down and restart your Surface.

The device may be in a “hung” state. Force the Surface to completely turn off all power and restart.

Performing a forced shutdown is like removing the battery from a laptop or unplugging a desktop PC. It resets the hardware and clears all the cache and memory for your hardware.

Unplug the Surface Dock.

Plug in the in-box PSU and confirm if the LED is lit.

If the in-box PSU charges the device for five minutes and you plug in the Dock and it does not charge, replace the Dock, as the Surface Connect on the Dock might be faulty.

If the in-box PSU does not charge the Surface, the issue likely does not concern the in-box PSU or the Dock and might be with the device itself. Try to troubleshoot the symptom on the Surface device.

It’s important that we test the Surface Device’s ability to charge using the in-box PSU.

Plug the USB device into a USB port on the Surface device.

This is to determine if the USB port on the Dock has the issue OR if the USB accessory that is being used has the issue.

The Surface Dock’s USB port supports standard USB power of 1.5 amps at 5 volts resulting in 7.5 watts (1.5 amp * 5 volt = 7.5 watts).

If the customer’s USB device requires more power, the USB device will not work.

Some devices, like DVD players have y-connectors, which means that they need to be plugged into two USB ports or have additional power input.

7. Try using a different USB device in the non-functioning port on the Dock.

Ask the customer to try another USB device such as a wired mouse, keyboard, or USB thumb drive on the non-functioning USB port on the Dock.

  1. If the other USB device works on the Dock: The Dock is not likely the issue.

  2. If the other USB device does not work on the Dock: The issue is potentially the Dock itself. Go to Step 8.

2. Check known issues.

From KB 4072657 Informational: Surface: Technical guides and known issues landing page, select the customer’s Surface model and check for the issue in the “Surface Dock and external displays” section. Check KB 3098748 Technical Guide: Surface: Surface Dock known issues.

 

There may be a current known issue that the customer is experiencing, with different troubleshooting steps.

Check known issues regularly.
3. Check if the issue occurs with a different physical dock. Check with the customer to see if this issue occurs with a different physical Surface Dock or if they have access to one to test with. It is important to determine if issue impacts one Surface Dock or multiple and if the customer has tested the issue with a different Surface dock.

4. Validate if the Surface can detect the monitor.

Connect the monitor directly to the Surface’s mini Display Port (mDP) and test the connection (bypassing the Dock).

After testing, connect the monitor back to the Dock.

This will determine if the issue concerns the Surface or the Dock. The external monitor should work when connected directly to the Surface.

5. Disconnect peripherals and reset power.

Do the following

  1. Disconnect the SurfaceLink connector from host.
  2. Disconnect all USB peripherals.
  3. Disconnect monitor connections on the Surface Dock.
  4. Unplug monitors from power and then plug them back in. Check to see if the monitors are plugged into a UPS or power strip. If so, try plugging the monitors directly into AC power.
  5. Unplug power cord. Note: Unplug power cord from the power brick. Do not just unplug power from the back power port of Dock. Check if it is plugged into UPS or power strip. If so, try plugging Surface Dock directly into power.
  6. Reconnect monitor connections.
  7. Connect SurfaceLink connector to host device.

This helps to determine if reseating the connections and power will help resolve issue.

6. Validate the customer’s monitors are compatible and configured to a compatible resolution and refresh rate.

Check your monitor’s compatibility.

The combination of the Surface device, plus the number of external displays, plus the refresh rate, plus the screen resolution plays a part in a given supported setup. For supported configurations see How many screens can I connect?

Daisy chaining scenarios only: Disconnect the daisy chain and guide the customer to use both mini Display Ports on the Surface Dock instead of daisy chaining.

Use the website and information on the support page to provide guidance to the customer.

7. Make sure that Windows recognizes the external monitors.
  1. Minimize all apps.
  2. Right-click the desktop background and select Display Settings.
  3. Scroll down to Multiple displays.
  4. First select Duplicate desktop on 1 (and 2). For multiple monitors, repeat step with desktop (1 and 3).
  5. Select Keep settings.
  6. Select Extend to this display.
  7. Select Keep settings.

This step may help the monitors and Windows enumerate correctly.

 

 

8. Change the refresh rate of the external display.
  1. Right-click the desktop background and select Display Settings.
  2. Select the external display.
  3. Scroll down and select Advanced display settings.
  4. Select Display adapter properties for Display 2 (and then repeat for Display 3 if two monitors are present).
  5. Select the Monitor tab.
  6. Change the Screen refresh rate from the menu. Try a lower refresh rate first.

If this resolves the issue, this means that the customer was trying to project to the external display at an unsupported resolution or refresh rate.

9. Clear the display cache.

(REMINDER! Do not use these steps if you are running Windows 10 S.)

  1. Undock the Surface from the Surface Dock.
  2. Download the Surface Dock registry file. Choose to Open the file.
  3. Select and run surface dock registry.reg
  4. Select Yes to allow changes to the Surface, select Yes to confirm, and then select OK to close.
  5. Restart the Surface, reconnect it to the Surface Dock, and try the external display again.

Note: If multiple-monitor configuration had been set up, the customer will have to re-enable the multimonitor configuration.

 

10. Run Windows Updates/Surface Diagnostic Tool (SDT).
  • English language support: Install and run Surface Diagnostic Tool. See KB 4014334 Procedure: Surface: Using the Microsoft Surface Diagnostic Toolkit.
  • Non-English support: Install all updates for Windows and Surface.

 

11. Run the Dock Updater Tool.

 

Guide the customer to follow the steps outlined at Microsoft Surface Dock Updater.

The direct link to the tool is www.microsoft.com/download/details.aspx?id=46703.

Make sure to Restart the device with the Dock connected.

 

12. Try a different cable OR adapter.

Cable:

If the monitor supports DisplayPort (DP), guide the customer to Use a Mini DisplayPort-to-DisplayPort cable.

Adapter:

If the monitor doesn’t support DisplayPort, we recommend using HDMI. Otherwise, use DVI or VGA. Direct the customer to Use an HDMI, DVI, or VGA adapter.

It may be that the customer’s cable or adapter is not functioning or is having issues.

It’s best to use the shortest cable length available. The shorter the cable, the stronger the signal between monitor and Dock.

 

Is the issue resolved?

Yes, close the case: Once resolved, have the customer check for updates.​​​​​​​

For SDT-enabled sites, tell the customer know about the Surface Diagnostic Toolkit.

For all other sites, check Settings > Update & Security > Windows Update for updates.

Making sure the Surface and Windows are up to date may help avoid this problem in the future.

No, replace the Dock: Set up an exchange for the customer based on the status of warranty.

Use KB 2791320 Informational: Surface: Exchange and fulfillment landing page.

There is likely a hardware problem with the Surface Dock or Surflink Connector.

9. Scaling – image too big or too small

Confirming the issue

When the customer has an external monitor connected and experiences any of the following:

  • Images are not the same size when moving windows from one monitor to another.
  • One display shows apps larger or smaller than other displays.
  • The Surface screen shows apps in a different resolution than is displayed on the external monitor.

Other descriptions and more information about scaling can be found at Windows scaling issues for high-DPI devices.

[Asset 4465463]

Confirmation/paraphrase

"Just to make sure I understand your issue; an app appears normal on Surface built-in display, but the content displayed on an external monitor is too large or too small."

How to code

After confirming the issue, code the case.

  • Choose the appropriate product > Accessories > Docking Station with Surface Connect > Scaling – Image Too Big or Too Small
