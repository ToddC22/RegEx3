Bank transfer through Internet banking

Definition
  • This service is usually provided by an integrator, which is an external company specialized in facilitating the interface between banks and the merchants (online shops).
  • They offer easy-to-use, full integration of the payment process in an online shop with a banking system.
  • When finalizing the purchase, all transfer data are completed automatically, and the editing is locked.
  • The buyer does not have to manually enter the purchase amount, order number, account number, name of the shop and other data required to make a payment, and therefore errors are not possible.
How it works
  1. Payment is made by offering customer the option to log in into their bank account using online banking credentials to authorize the payment.
  2. Upon purchase, the consumer logs into their own online bank and authorizes the payment.
  3. The funds are directly debited from their bank account in real-time, and the merchant will receive an immediate payment confirmation.
  4. Account access and transaction validation is performed using same security process offered by the bank.

Credit/Debit card

Definition
  • A bank card, credit or debit, is typically a plastic card issued by a bank/financial institution to its clients that performs one or more of a number of services that relate to giving the client access to funds, either from the client's own bank account, or through a credit account.
  • "Virtual" cards provide most of the same functions but exist only electronically, usually requiring a device, such as a smart phone, for purchases in physical locations.
  • Physically, a bank card will usually have the card holder's name, the issuer's name, a unique 16-digit card number, and expiration date printed on it, a magnetic strip on the back and/or a chip and/or contactless features (NFC) on the front, enabling various machines to read and access information.

Additional security is provided by:

  • personal identification number (PIN) code known by client and used when performing "card present" transactions (when physical card is used) at an automated teller machine (ATM) or point of sale (POS)
  • card security code (CSC), also called card verification value (CVV), card verification number, card verification code (CVC), card verification data (CVD), verification code (v-code) or signature panel code (SPC) that acts as a security feature for "card not present" payment transactions (online, phone payment, and so on). The card security code is typically the three or four digits printed, not embossed like the card number, on the signature strip on the back (Discover, MC, Visa) or front (AmEx) of the card.
Differentiation: Credit cards and debit cards

Credit cards and debit cards typically look almost identical and are used in the same way. The difference between them is the way the customer gets the money:

  • Credit cards are lines of credit. When you use a credit card, the issuer puts money toward the transaction. This is a loan the customer is expected to pay back in full (usually within 30 days), to avoid interest charges. Spending is limited to the available credit limit. Daily or per transaction spending limits can be set depending on each financial institution's rules.
  • Charge cards are lines of credit where the cardholder is obligated to repay the debt to the card issuer in full by the due date, usually on a monthly basis, or be subject to late fees and restrictions on further card use. Charge cards are distinct from credit cards in that credit cards are revolving credit instruments that do not need to be paid in full every month and a balance may be carried over, on which an interest is paid. Charge cards are typically issued without spending limits, but credit cards usually have a specified credit limit that the cardholder may not exceed.
  • Debit cards provide access to your funds. Any time you use a debit card to buy something, money is deducted from your account. With a debit card, you can only spend the money you have available to you. Spending limit is usually related to amount available in the account, unless lower daily- or transaction-based maximum limits are set, depending on each financial institution's rules and/or customer preferences. Some debit cards also have overdraft enabled, allowing customers to exceed their account amount under certain conditions defined by each financial institution.
  • Prepaid credit and debit cards are functionally similar, however prepaid cards require the card holder to fund the account prior to use, may have usage restrictions enabled (for example, disabled or limited online purchasing) and may not be associated to an established financial institution account.
How it works
  • Customer bank/financial institution provides cards to the end user.
  • Online store bank/financial institution provides the infrastructure to merchants to process the payments using cards, and
  • The payment system/network provider/payment scheme (for example, Visa, Mastercard, AmEx, and so on) that owns the card brand and the networks that allows that specific brand transactions to be processed.
  • Payment systems are global or regional/local, such as Union Pay. Each payment network provider has a set of rules that govern the participating financial institutions in its payment system, both as issuer and acquirer.
  • Rules define how a card holder must be identified for security, how transactions may be denied by the bank and how banks may cooperate for fraud prevention and the like.
  • When debit cards are used to secure a transaction, such as a device exchange, customer funds for the cost of the replacement device may be held in escrow pending the completion of the transaction (hold amount), and are released upon completion, such as the receipt of the ticketed device in the case of an advance exchange. Credit cards will only be charged in case of default on said exchange (customer does not return the ticketed device or returns it after the return period ).
How it works: Pre-authorization credit card charge

In some markets, whenever a customer makes a change to the credit card information on their account or attempts to make a purchase from an online retailer, a small temporary charge is made to their credit card. This charge is equivalent to 1 USD in the customer’s local currency. This charge ensures that the card used on the account is valid. The charge is usually removed within 3-5 days.

These charges may appear on a customer's online statement when a customer:

  • Adds a new payment option or updates an existing card on file
  • Tries to purchase a subscription, and the transaction fails
  • Asks an agent to add a new card to an account, and the update is not successful

Authorization holds are a standard process in the credit card industry. Funds are not removed from the customer's account. If the customer has questions or concerns about an authorization charge, direct the customer to contact the financial institution for a complete explanation.

Direct debit (asynchronous)

Definition

  • A direct debit or direct withdrawal is a financial transaction in which one person (or company) withdraws funds from another person's bank account.

  • Before the payer's banker will allow the transaction to take place, the payer must have advised the bank that he or she has authorized the payee to directly withdraw the funds.

  • Direct debits are made under each country's rules and are usually restricted to domestic transactions in those countries. An exception in this respect is the Single Euro Payments Area (SEPA) which allows for Euro-denominated cross-border (and domestic) direct debits.
How it works
  1. To set up payments by direct debit, the payer must provide a Direct Debit agreement to the merchant. This instruction contains bank-approved wording that makes it clear the payer is setting up an ongoing authority for the merchant to debit their account. The interface for completing the Direct Debit agreement is controlled by the merchant, who then sends the data from the form to the customer's bank.
  2. Before a company or organization can collect payment by Direct Debit they need to be approved by a bank or use a commercial bureau/service provider (for example, Trustly) to collect direct debit payments on their behalf.
  3. The Direct Debit agreement can either be included in the transactions process or can be created as a standalone feature from a Microsoft account.

In the United States, direct debit usually means an Automated Clearing House (ACH) transfer from a bank account to a biller, initiated by the biller.

Direct debit is an asynchronous payment method, as additional steps are required in the backend to settle the transaction for Direct Debit agreement settling, initial and subsequent transactions.

e-Commerce payment services

Definition
  • e-Commerce payment service providers are third-party companies that specialize in providing and intermediating payment between an end-user (consumer) and a merchant. They allow customers to make purchases online without providing their payment details to the merchants.
  • They act as on online gateway between the consumer and various payment methods, such as cards, bank transfers, and account balance with third party company, that are either offered directly (for example INCIS) or managed by consumer in a e-wallet (such as PayPal and Alipay).
  • Account creation with the third-party company is required (for example, PayPal and Alipay).

INICIS can no longer be added or modified as a Payment Instrument in Microsoft Store/Commerce platform. Customers who already have INICIS associated to their Microsoft account can continue to use.

How it works
  1. During a transaction, the consumer selects the third-party supplier from list of payment methods and a form specific to that supplier automatically opens.
  2. Transaction details are displayed and the consumer walks through an authentication/validation process specific to that payment company.

Mobile operator billing

Definition

Mobile operator billing, also called direct mobile billing or direct-to-bill, is a method of paying for merchandise by charging the purchase to a cellular telephone (mobile phone) account.

How it works

Carrier billing (or direct operator billing) is a mobile payment method that bills purchases from third party vendors of digital products. Carrier billing typically uses a two-factor authentication method. The user enters his mobile number and receives a one-time password via text message to complete credentials and the transaction.

Online currency/cryptocurrency

Definition

An online currency is a digital asset that works as a payment method under certain conditions. It can be either a currency obtained and used with a certain merchant only (for example, CSV for Microsoft Store) or an alternative currency that is wide-spread and used for electronic transactions.

How it works

Merchants that have this option enabled would allow consumer to select that specific PI or to deposit amounts in a preset account available with the merchant. During purchase flow consumer would get the option to use online currency and/or combine it with standard payment methods.

Refund & Returns Guidelines Refunds for ACH cannot be processed until the funds have cleared the customer’s bank account and have been credited to Microsoft. If the customer requests a chargeback via their bank and requests a refund from Microsoft before the funds have settled, this could result in either double charges to the customer or double refunds. Because MST does not show when this settlement happens, agents must wait 8 days from the date of purchase before processing a refund for any ACH purchases. Example: The purchase was made on Feb 01. The customer contacts support on Feb 05 for a refund. Agent will follow up with customer on Feb 09 for an ACH refund - The waiting period is from time of purchase, not time of support contact.

If the customer requests a chargeback via their bank and receives a refund from Microsoft before the funds have settled, this could result in either double charges to the customer or double refunds. Because MST does not show when this settlement happens, advocates must wait 10 days from the date of purchase before processing a refund for any SEPA purchases.

Example: The purchase was made on Feb 01. The customer contacts support on Feb 05 for a refund. The advocate will follow up with customer on Feb 11 for a SEPA refund - The waiting period is from time of purchase, not time of support contact.
