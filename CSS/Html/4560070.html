1. Overview

Thousands of consumer support advocates help our customers overcome challenges daily, and while the reasons our customers contact us may differ, how we manage their cases should not. Customers that contact Microsoft should receive the same effective support experience that is consistent and on brand, regardless of their product or service problem, or where they contact us from. Support consistency improves the support experience for our customers, enabling you make our customers fans of Microsoft products and services across the globe.

Consumer Case Management Framework (CMF) is being launched, to bring consistency and structure to supporting our consumer customers by:

  • Aligning the way our customer’s cases are managed end to end by reducing the process variance that exists today.
  • Standardizing on core process steps and associated sub process steps.
  • Using consistent terminology in our processes.
  • Establishing an effective measurement platform to report on CMF impact and progress.
  • Representing One Microsoft to our customers – Keeping the customer at the center of everything we do.

CMF comprises four components:

  • Behaviors: Expected behaviors that need to be exhibited throughout the customer interaction.
  • Process: Five core processes steps (areas) with 21 sub process steps (procedures) that detail how you deliver consumer customer support.
  • Measurement: Customer support impact will be measured through Interaction Analytics (IAP) Customer Satisfaction (CSAT) and Customer Sentiment analysis.
  • Governance: Continue to use Speak Up! to submit process questions and improvement ideas.

CMF has been designed to:

  • Provide you with process handling instructions that conform to the natural flow of the contact, to make it clear what support steps to take at specific points.
  • Introduce a standard approach on how we manage and resolve customer issues using a common language to describe the work we do.
  • Consolidate procedures and terminology to eliminate duplication and variance.
  • Focus on core case management steps only to allow flexibility for LOB’s to retain some non-core process steps specific to their business.
  • Introduce effective change management to ensure the customer support process is always current and takes account of your feedback.
  • Highlight the expected advocate behaviors you should demonstrate. See KB 4552164 Procedure: CMF: Expected advocate behaviors for Consumer CMF.

As CMF builds on existing support procedures, there will be parts of the Framework that you are already familiar with and parts that are new and will change how you conduct your case management work.

3. How to approach CMF

3.1 Core process steps (Areas)

CMF is divided into five core process steps that you will readily identify with.

  1. Greet and establish communication.
  • Start a conversation with the customer and setting the correct tone for the rest of the interaction.
  1. Identify customer need.
  • Reach a mutual agreement with the customer on what issues you will be solving together and if they must pay a fee for this support interaction.
  1. Solution discovery/develop solution.
  • Find a solution to the issue at hand, using all approved resources to search information, such as Service Desk Knowledge, and Wiki’s.
  1. Deliver solution/verify solution.
  • Deliver an agreed solution for the customer, setting expectations, and validating that the resolution addresses the customer’s request/issue.
  1. Close.
  • Ensure that the case closing conditions are met and actions are taken to close the case with customer’s permission.

3.2 Sub process steps (Procedures)

Beneath the five-core process steps, a further 21 sub process steps are detailed in the framework. These sub process steps may be specific to one core process area or transverse two or more core process steps. For example, greeting the customer will only occur under the first core process pillar, while coding the interaction and documenting the actions taken should occur across all five core process steps.

See the illustrative table below.

[Asset 4560988]

Sub process step

Details

1. Accept support request

Accept the support request per tool requirements in a prepared manner by reviewing case notes.

2. Greet customer – Confirm request

Greet your customer in a professional manner, noting authentication status while respecting regional requirements and norms.

3. Open case (new/existing)

Determine if the customer is calling for a new or an existing case.

4. Ghost contact

Handling the customer interaction, if customer has not responded to your initial greeting.

5. Account verification

Speak to the correct owner (validation) of the Microsoft account to authenticate the customer.

6. Entitlement

Determine if the customer has the right to receive free support or is required pay for this support incidence.

7. Review customer history

Review the customer history to provide additional context to the conversation, enabling you to deliver a personalized support.

8. Case ownership

What is expected of you during the case ownership step of case management.

9. Scoping

Reach mutual agreement with the customer on the service request you are solving together in a free or paid for support scenario.

10. Support boundaries

Determine support boundaries to help identify whether the product is actively supported by Microsoft or not and whether the support request falls into your area of expertise.

11. Transfer

Transfer the customer to another Microsoft support department or skill type if solution resides outside of your support boundaries.

12. Form solution

Find solutions in reference materials (approved resources such as SDK) that matches the identified scenario.

13. Escalate

Where assistance is required to progress a case with a higher tier. Case ownership should be transferred to a resource with specialized skills or technical depth expertise.

14. Deliver/validate

Provide the best solution to the customer based on solution discovery steps (information research) and validate with the customer that the solution addresses the request/issue.

15. Close case

Follow up on open commitments prior to closing the case with customer’s approval.

16. Call Back

Call-back process which is useful for various support situations, such as unexpected disconnects or where collection of diagnostic data is required or where a call back is requested by the customer.

17. Case documentation/case coding

Taking specific notes and documenting the actions taken during the support interaction is important and how accurate coding helps drive product improvements and a better customer experience.

18. Collaborate/consult

Whenever you are blocked in progressing a support request to resolution, you may collaborate/consult with others to get help.

Collaboration/consult can be informal or formal (child SR, Task, Work Item) but does not require transferring case ownership.

19. Dropped contact

What to do when a call is disconnected unexpectedly.

20. Unresponsive customers

What to do when the customer stops responding during a live conversation, or when you call the customer at the promised time and they do not answer.

21. Tools down

What to do when tools are down.
