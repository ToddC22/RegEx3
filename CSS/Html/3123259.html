1. Process

1.1 About the "Recover your Microsoft account" form

The Recover your Microsoft account form, also known as the Automated Customer Service Rep (ACSR) form, is a process customers can use to regain access to a Microsoft account when they have also lost access to all their security info and did not save a recovery code for that account.

This form relies on the customer remembering several details about their account usage that can be difficult to remember, so this process should only be used after all other attempts to regain access to an account have failed.

Note: Be aware that customers who have Two Step Verification (TSV) enabled will not be able to recover their account by going through the Recovery Form. Follow KB 2984708 Procedure: Microsoft account: Two-step verification (TSV) contacts instead.

1.2 When to use the "Recover your Microsoft account" form

Only use this process when you have exhausted the guidance outlined for one of the following Microsoft account states and the customer still cannot regain access to their account because they do not have access to their security info or a recovery code:

Confirm the account
  1. Ask them to access account.live.com/pw and provide the email address, phone number, or username for the account that customers are trying to recover.
  2. Ask the customer to review the security information that is listed on this account.
    • If customers do not recognize the phone number or email listed, they may be using the wrong email or phone number. They can check to see if this is the right account by requesting a username hint using the security phone number or email associated to your account.
    • If the security contact information listed is familiar, but they no longer have access to it, select I don’t have any of these and select Next.

Important notes: Advocates should never fill out this form for the customer!
If the customer asks why the advocates can't just fix this problem or asks to engage, inform the customer that:
"This is the only way to get back into your account when your security info won’t work. We do not have that kind of access to your account for privacy and security reasons, so if this process doesn’t work, you may have to create a new Microsoft account instead."
Note: For compromised accounts, refer to KB 930018 Procedure: How to handle account takeover on a Microsoft account. If an account is compromised, Password Reset and ACSR attempts from the customer will not be successful.

1.3 Talking points

The following table provides talking points that should be used for every case in which a customer requests a password reset. Go through these questions with each customer as part of troubleshooting prior to any incidence of engagement.

  1. Set expectations: Password reset is not guaranteed.
  2. Filling out the ACSR as completely as possible with the information on account at time of creation/last update increases the likelihood of account recovery.
  3. Suggest creating a new account to the customer as an alternative, especially for those with few purchases, young accounts, or accounts with few Microsoft services.
  4. Creating a new account will take less time than an engagement and is guaranteed to succeed.
Question for customer: Advocate talking points

Why are you trying to get back into your account?

Is the customer trying to play Xbox with a friend? What’s lead to the customer contacting support today?

What have you done so far?

Review customer’s case history. If an advocate has not guided them through the password reset and security information process, do so first by following KB 2984712 Procedure: Microsoft account: Password reset. Many customers struggle with different parts of the reset process and resetting the password through those means is always preferable.

How many times have you tried to use this form?

If the customer has used the account recovery form multiple times and is absolutely sure they cannot use security info, then walk them through filling out a form per Section 2 and the best practices listed in this table.

Note: Customers can submit this twice per day.

Did you fill out the ACSR on a PC that you normally use with your Microsoft account?

The odds of passing increase if the customer fills out this form from a computer that has successfully signed into that account previously.

Did you fill out the ACSR on a network on which you’ve successfully signed in with your Microsoft account before?

The odds of passing also increase if the customer goes through this process from a network location (For example, Home Wi-Fi or work network) where a previous sign-in occurred.

What information did you fill out on the form?

While customers should fill out as many fields as possible, even if they are not 100% sure or accurate, they do not need to fill out the whole form, just the fields that they know apply to their account. Inform the customer that filling out more information on the form increases their chance of passing the password reset request.

What Microsoft services do you use with your Microsoft account?

For example, the email section of the form should only be filled out if the customer uses an Outlook or Hotmail email account. Customers with third-party accounts should leave those parts blank.

How much content have you purchased on this account?

If the customer has not purchased much content, suggest that it may be easier to create a new account than to reset the password. Resetting the password will take the extra time of an engagement and the possibility of being denied the reset.

How long have you had the account?

If the account was created recently, ask if the customer would consider creating a new account.

When was the last time the account was used?

If the account has been inactive for 6+ months, the likelihood of account recovery is very low. Suggest creating a new account as an alternative.

After several attempts at passing the ACSR form, would you be OK with creating a new account?

Customers can submit the ACSR form until they pass it. However, if they wish, you can guide them through creating a new account after failing ACSR several times. It is up to the customer if they want to keep trying, even after they create a new account. If the customer has little invested in this account in terms of purchases, services, or Gamerscore, creating a new account should be a suggested option rather than an engagement.

Use KB 3107652 Procedure: Microsoft account: Customer cannot regain access to account to guide the process of creating a new Microsoft account.

1.4 Coding the Incident form

Pointing the customer to the ACSR form should NOT be interpreted as a Redirect, Referral or Transfer taking place. A Redirect, Referral or Transfer requires you to get the customer in touch with another person or another entity, directly or indirectly. Filling an online form out does not fit this definition.

Where available, code Solution Delivered to “Educated Customer”.

2. Customer experience

2.1 Using the "Recover your Microsoft account" form

The "Recover your Microsoft account" form is the final path that customers can take to regain access to a Microsoft account. However, because of the complexity involved, customers should be strongly encouraged to try to use their security info before going through this process. The steps located at https://support.microsoft.com/help/17875/microsoft-account-recover begin after a customer receives an interrupt that prompts them to verify their identity when attempting to sign-in to a Microsoft account and the customer does not have access to their security info or a recovery code.

3. Things to try

3.1 What if the customer doesn't get a reply after submitting the form?

While most responses will come within a few minutes, it can take up to 24 hours for a reply. If the customer has already waited 24 hours, here’s some things to try:
  1. Make sure the customer is using an email address they can access that is not the email address of the account they are trying to sign in to.
  2. Have the customer check their junk folder for the email message.
  3. Suggest trying a different email address if possible
  4. Try creating a free outlook.com email account to receive the email, which can then be used as security info if they get back into their account or as their new account if they cannot.

3.2 Next steps

After explaining what the follow-up process will be, have the customer check to see if they already received an email response. While responses can take up to 24 hours, most customers will receive an email within a couple of minutes.


If the customer received a rejection, they can try the process again if they feel that they remembered or could do some research to answer the questions on the form, (like contacting friends or family for the subject lines of emails they recently sent if they have an Outlook/Hotmail account). If we've assisted the customer in filling out the form and they have no new info, we should recommend they create a new account.

Note: ACSR considers whether the profile information entered in the form matches what we have on record. However, since profile information is readily discoverable online, ACSR takes other factors about the request into account as well. Profile information alone is insufficient to grant the password reset.
