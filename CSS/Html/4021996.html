What is this article about?

This article provides guidance and handling for frustrated customers who are requesting to be escalated to a “manager” or “supervisor”.
Note: This information is specific to escalation from Tier 1 to Tier 2.

1. Identifying & managing customer frustration

1.1. Identifying customer frustration

Understanding how to acknowledge, empathize and address a customer’s frustration is key to helping ease these feelings and resolve the customer’s underlying issues. You may recognize increasing customer frustration as:

  • Expressed dissatisfaction with a product – it may be with a previous or a current Microsoft Support experience.
  • Requests to speak to another agent, manager, supervisor, or a Microsoft executive.
  • Verbal cues:
    • Personal insults
    • Profanity
    • Racial, ethnic, religious, or other insults
    • Yelling
  • Unreasonable demands.
  • Threats of violence against themselves, others, a public location, or Microsoft:
    See KB 2626175 Procedure: Handling abusive customers for more information.
  • Threats to sue Microsoft or requests to contact Microsoft legal department:
    See KB     2629445 Procedure: Legal: Assisting customers requesting for a legal contact or arbitration for more information.

1.2 Best practices for managing customer frustration

  • Don’t take it personally – the customer’s emotions are not directed toward you at all.
  • Stay calm:
    • Do not reply to them in an emotional manner.
    • Remember that you are a professional within your team and Microsoft and are there to help.
  • Acknowledge their frustration.
  • Give them an opportunity to vent if needed.
  • Paraphrase their concern in your response, this will help to make sure they feel heard and understood.
  • Let them know you are here to help.
  • Assure them that we will work towards resolving their problem.
  • Offer only what you know can be reasonably be done for them.
  • Remember there is a right way, as well as a wrong way to say "no".
  • By being truthful along with consistently following our policies, we can avoid setting any unrealistic expectations.
  • Provide closure:
    • Be clear about what will be done and when it will be done.
    • The customer should know that there is a plan, and what they should expect.

1.3 Managing customer frustration

When you recognize an “at risk” customer, you should take ownership of recovering the customer before escalating:

  1. Listen: Listen to them so that you can understand why they are contacting us.
  2. Ask clarifying questions: Ask questions to better understand where their experience differed from their expectations.
  3. Check understanding: Confirm their experience and expectations for closure or resolution.
  4. Empathize: Relate an understanding of their frustration with sensitivity.
  5. Offer assurance of assistance: Take responsibility and let them know that you are there to help. This may not necessarily mean that you are able to fix the problem, but that you’re identifying who can do what they need done or who can help with understanding why a particular outcome occurred.
  6. Gauge frustration: Is there an opportunity to recover the customer or should they be escalated immediately?
  7. Present Action: Propose an action plan to the customer and gain agreement. Be careful to set proper expectations and not over-commit.
  8. Recap: Confirm the agreed upon actions with the customer and set expectations for the next steps.

2. Methods for recovering customer engagement

  1. Maintain a calm, even tone. Try dropping the volume of your voice slightly over time to encourage the customer to focus on listening you.
  2. Suggested verbiage:
    • "(Customer’s name), I am here to help you."
    • "(Customer’s name), thank you for allowing me to help you with this issue.”
    • "(Customer’s name), I understand that you are frustrated. Let’s work together to take care of this."

      If a customer becomes abusive:
    • "(Customer’s name), I find your comment offensive. For us to best help you, I ask that we keep this call professional."
    • "(Customer’s name), I know you have had a bad experience to this point. I want to work with you to change that."
    • "(Customer’s name), please do not swear at me. I understand your frustration, but let’s focus on resolving this problem."
  3. Never refuse to escalate a customer to a supervisor or manager.

4. Supervisor/manager recovery process

Before you transfer a customer to a supervisor:

  1. Attempt to recover the customer (see Recovery Steps above).
  2. Ask to place the customer on hold.
  3. Follow your site or supplier’s process to transfer a customer to a supervisor.

5. Complaints about products/services

Refer customer to provide direct feedback using below method for these products/services:

  • Windows/Surface: Feedback Hub on Windows 10 (Type “Feedback” on Cortana/Search box), see Send feedback to Microsoft with the Feedback Hub app: https://support.microsoft.com/help/4021566
  • Feedback Hub - Windows app on Microsoft Store:
    https://www.microsoft.com/store/p/feedback-hub/9nblggh4r32n
  • Office: User Voice:
    https://support.office.com/article/How-do-I-give-feedback-on-Microsoft-Office-2b102d44-b43f-4dd2-9ff4-23cf144cfb11
  • Skype: Skype Community or Help Menu (Log in to Skype, open the “Help Menu” and then select “Give Feedback”)
  • Xbox: Complaints should be directed to support.xbox.com or handled by advocates, however product suggestions can be filed https://aka.ms/xboxrequests by customers.
