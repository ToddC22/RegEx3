1. Overview

1.1 Why is this happening?

The customer is unable to log into their Microsoft account because they are either entering the wrong account name, the wrong password, or both.

Return to top
 

1.2 How do I fix this?

First, verify that the customer is attempting to sign into the correct account. Incorrect password problems are sometimes caused by customers who are accidentally trying to sign into the wrong account. After verifying the account name is correct, walk the customer through the password reset process.

This process should only be used for phone or chat support. Microsoft Store Answer Desk Techs cannot reset or remove a password in store, even if the customer created an appointment.

2. Troubleshooting

2.1 Ensure account name is correct

Check Assisted Support Desktop (ASD). Does the information the customer provided match what ASD shows?

If ASD information does not match customer information:

If ASD information does not match customer information:

1. Ask:

Are you sure this is the correct email address for your Microsoft account?

If the answer is not 100% positive, refer to KB 4479728 Procedure: Microsoft account: Forgot my username and see if there is a way that they can find the right account name by asking someone they emailed recently or checking with a relative?

2. Ask:

When was the last time you signed into this account?

  • If the last time was a few years ago:
    • Accounts are automatically deleted if they aren't used for more than a few years. So, it's possible that your account may have been deleted.
    • Depending on the last date the customer used their Microsoft account, it may have been deleted for inactivity. If so, the customer will have to create a new account and will not have access to any of their old apps, data, or services. See KB 2979990 Procedure: Microsoft account: Handling "That Microsoft account doesn't exist" error for more information.
  • If the last time was over 60 days:
    • Did you request to close this account? Accounts can be reopened up to 60 days after a close request is made, but after that they are permanently deleted.
    • If the customer did initiate a request to close their account over 60 days ago, they will need to create a new Microsoft account. See KB 2979990 for more information.

3. Ask:

Have any of the following occurred?

  • They reported unknown charges from Microsoft.
  • They reported a different Microsoft account sign in address than what is currently on the account.
  • They do not recognize any of the security information associated with the account.
  1. If they answered no to all three questions, proceed directly to section 2.2 Use your tools.
  2. If the customer answered yes to any of the three questions, the password reset process must be put on hold and the customer needs to be taken through the appropriate troubleshooting related to Account Takeover (ATO) KB 930018 Procedure: How to handle account takeover on a Microsoft account. Confirm with the customer that you want to ensure their account security is a top priority and will take the necessary steps to see if they need to be engaged for password reset or account takeover.

4. After all questions and any troubleshooting provided in previous steps have been completed:

  • If ATO evidence is not found – ensure you note that you checked and found NO ATO present on the account. After which proceed with password reset process below.
  • If UA evidence is found – Do not escalate as password reset, follow the Account Takeover engagement scenario guidance in KB 817557 Guideline: Advanced Capability Advocate engagement. 

If ASD information matches the customer's information:

Check for any typos or mistakes in the account name the customer entered in the sign in screen. Have them select Sign in with a different Microsoft account at the sign in screen and retype their account name and repeat it to you. Then check what they repeat against the masked information in ASD.

2.2 Use your tools

Lookup the account in ASD and validate you are seeing account details that match.
 
  • Ask about any subscriptions they have or have had (Xbox Live, Microsoft 365, Groove). Do you see these subscriptions on the account?
  • Have they ever contacted support before? Do you see any previous service requests (SR) associated to the account?


Open the Microsoft account status section and review the customer details.

[Asset 4480994]

  • Look at the account created date – Was the account created more recently than the last successful login date? Is the customer talking about a new account, but this one is years old?

Customers memory may not be perfect for some of this information, but based on the answers given, you can decide to spend more or less time on validating that you are looking at the correct account.

2.3 Ensure that the customer's password is correct

Ask:
 
"Could you make sure your caps-lock isn’t on and carefully try to type your password in again to make sure there weren’t any errors?"

Review the Microsoft account status section: has the customer changed their password recently? If so, ask them if they’ve changed their password recently. If the Microsoft account status section is showing a recent change, and the customer does not remember doing so, there could be several reasons:
 
  • We are working on the wrong account.
  • They share their account with someone and they changed the password.
  • Their account has been compromised.
  • They simply don’t remember making the change.
     

Review the security information with the customer, as this will help you understand if you are in the correct account. Then ask probing questions to determine which of the other scenarios you are dealing with.

Note: Never suggest that their account has been compromised unless you have strong evidence that it has been.

If the customer is reasonably make sure they entered the correct account name and password, continue to section 2.4. Even if they say they have already done this make sure to guide them through the process. Many customers get tripped up at one of the steps without realizing the error.

3.1 Account aged out

If a customer has not signed in for a significant period of time, their account may be closed due to inactivity. To find out if this may have happened, ask:

When is the last time you signed in with this account?
Scenario Advocate messaging Action
If last sign in was before June 2014: "Accounts are automatically deleted when they are inactive for a certain period of time. I’m sorry, but It sounds like your account may have been deleted due to inactivity." If an account is deleted, the only option is to create a new account. 
If last sign in
was over 60
days ago:		 "Is it possible that you requested to have your account closed? If so, accounts can be re-opened within 60 days of that request but are permanently closed after that." If the account was closed, then the customer will have to create a new account.
If last sign in
was less than
60 days ago:		 This account probably wasn’t closed or deleted. Continue to other steps.


Prior to June 2014, accounts were automatically closed if there is no sign in activity for more than one year. In June 2014, this time period was extended to two years. Then, on August 2015, it was extended to five years.

Accounts that age out due to inactivity are permanently deleted.

3.2 Closed accounts

When an account is marked for closure by the customer, the account is kept in a marked for deletion state for 60 days. After 60 days, the account can no longer be recovered and is deleted. At that point, the customer will have to create a new Microsoft account.
 
  • App purchases generally cannot be recovered, but there may be some things advocates can do for customers who had services and apps tied to a closed account. Refer to KB 2974112 Informational: Microsoft account Closure FAQ: All Products for more information.
