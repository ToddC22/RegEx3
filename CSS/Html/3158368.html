Technical Guide: Surface: Power supply unit issues

[Asset 4049349]

Who should use this article?

  • Surface

What is this article about?

  • This article will help you address common Surface power supply (PSU) issues that your customers may encounter. This article should be used any time you work with a customer to resolve Surface PSU issues. If the customer uses an RRT keyword at any time during your interaction, follow the appropriate RRT process in KB 2764515 Procedure: Surface & Xbox: Handling Product Safety (RRT) Contacts.

1. Cable issue/damage

Confirming the issue

Customers may contact support with issues pertaining to the wall to PSU(AC) or PSU to Surface (DC) cables of the PSU.

Issues could include the following:

  • Damage to connector

  • Exposed wires or sheathing

  • Crimped or bent cables

  • Damage to AC plug

    [Asset 4338133]

  • Wall to PSU (AC)
  • PSU To Surface (DC)

Note: If the customer refers to the cord replacement program refer to section 2.

Confirmation/paraphrase

“Just to make sure I understand your issue: You have some damage or issues with the PSU cords and/or connectors.”

How to code

After confirming the issue, code the case.

  • Damage to wall to PSU(AC)
  • Surface Device > Battery and charging > Power Supply-Surface > cable issue/damage-Wall to PSU-AC
  • Damage to PSU to Surface(DC)
  • Surface Device > Battery and charging > Power Supply-Surface > cable issue/damage-PSU to Surface-DC

3. Disconnect the PSU and check connections.

[Asset 4338130]

  • A: Unplug the power connector from side of device.
  • B: Unplug the PSU from the wall outlet.
  • C: Check that the power cord is securely attached to the PSU.
  • D: Unplug any USB devices from the charge port on the PSU (Not available on all PSUs).
  • A: Wipe the pins on the power connector with a soft, dry, lint-free cloth (microfiber cloths work great).
  • B: Plug the PSU back into the wall outlet. If possible, try a different wall outlet.
  • A: Plug the power connector back into side of device.

Resets PSU and verifies all connections are secure.

 

Dirt or debris on the connector’s pins could be preventing a power connection.

4.  Check the PSU and cables for any physical damage.

Ask the customer to check PSU and cables for any frayed or damage. See KB 2791320 Informational: Surface: Exchange and fulfillment landing page for information on customer induced damage and warranty.

Damaged PSU or cables may cause the PSU to not function as intended.
5. Determine whether the charger is the in-box charger, a different Surface charger, or a third-party charger.

Ask if the customer is using the charger/power supply unit (PSU) that came with their Surface, a different Surface PSU, or a third-party PSU. If they are not sure, direct the customer to take a photo of the back of the PSU block and send it to you. There are several methods to identify the type of PSU, including:

  • The model number is listed – see #1 in the image below.
  • Depending on the PSU, the wattage of the PSU may be listed the bottom also - #2 below.
     

A Microsoft PSU should contain icons/certifications similar to the following image:

[Asset 4465199]

It’s important to determine if the PSU was supplied by Microsoft or a third-party.

Note: If it’s a third-party PSU, advise the customer to contact that manufacturer.

If the customer is not using the Surface PSU that came with their device, they may need to switch to the correct PSU. For more information, see How to charge Surface.

Is the issue resolved?

Yes, close the case: Once resolved, have the customer check for updates.

 

For SDT enabled sites, let the customer know about the Surface Diagnostic Toolkit.

For all other sites, check Settings > Update & Security > Windows Update for updates.

KB 4014334 Procedure: Surface: Using the Microsoft Surface Diagnostic Toolkit.

Making sure the Surface and Windows are up to date may help avoid this problem in the future.

No, replace the device: Set up an exchange for the customer based on the status of their warranty.

Use KB 2791320 Informational: Surface: Exchange and fulfillment landing page  landing page to create a service order.

There is likely a hardware problem with the Surface device.

2. Customer needs help with the Power Cord Replacement program

Confirming the issue

The customer has received an email informing them of a power cord recall or browsed the page Microsoft AC power cord recall for original Surface Pro, Surface Pro 2, and certain Surface Pro 3 devices.

Confirmation/paraphrase

How to code

After confirming the issue, code the case.

3. Tip does not attach properly/tip damaged

Confirming the issue

The customer may contact support with one or more of the following issues with the PSU tip.

  • PSU tip is damaged.

  • PSU tip is not seating properly or making complete connection.

  • The housing of the PSU tip is frayed, damaged, bent, broke, or has exposed wires.

“Just to make sure I understand your issue: The PSU tip does not attach or there is some damage to it.”

4. Problems with PSU’s USB charge port

Confirming the issue

When the customer plugs in a USB device to the USB port on the PSU it doesn’t charge.

Confirmation/paraphrase

“Just to make sure I understand your issue: When you plug in your USB device to the USB port on the PSU, the USB device does not charge.”

How to code

After confirming the issue, code the case.

  • Surface Device > Battery and charging > Power Supply -Surface > Problems with USB port

Resets PSU and verifies all connections are secure.

Dirt or debris on the connector’s pins could be preventing a power connection.

4. Test with another charger.

Have customer plug the USB accessory into another USB charger to test if it charges there.

To determine if the device charges plugged into wall outlet directly. 

Note: Some devices may require more power than the USB port can supply (5 volts at 1 amp for total of 5 watts). 

Some Surface Book and Book 2 PSU supply 5 volts at 1.5 amps for total of 7.5 watts. Check PSU for exact specs.

5. Attempt to charge another device using same USB cable.

Have customer locate a different USB device and plug into PSU with same cable.

If another device charges with the same cable, then issue is with original device that customer was trying to charge. This could also indicate that the device requires more power than the port can supply.

6. Replace USB cable.

Have customer replace the USB cable with another known good USB cable to see if device charges.

To determine if the issue is with the USB cable.

6. PSU brick – hot/thermal

Confirming the issue

When the customer touches the Surface PSU brick, they notice it is warm or hot.

Confirmation/paraphrase

"Just to make sure I understand your issue, you are concerned with the PSU being warm or hot to the touch. When the PSU is charging or under high work load it is normal for the Surface PSU to be warm to the touch and it is designed to operate well with safety limits but let's take a look at few things to verify it is working properly.”

How to code

After confirming the issue, code the case.

  • Surface Device > Battery and charging > Power Supply – Surface > PSU Brick – Hot/Thermal

7. Physical damage to micro USB tip or USB tip (Surface 3 only)

Confirming the issue

Customer indicates there is damage to the Surface 3 charger such as

  • The Surface 3 PSU micro USB or USB connector has issues seating into the Surface due to connector damage.

  • The plastic housing of the micro USB or USB connector is damaged, broken, or has exposed wires.

  • Other damage specific to the tip or connector on either ends of the USB cable.

Confirmation/paraphrase

"Just to make sure I understand your issue, you have some physical damage to the Surface 3 charger.”
