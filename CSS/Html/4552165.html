1. General

Q: How are you ensuring all employees are safe?

A: The health and safety of our employees is a top priority at Microsoft. To ensure the safety of our staff around the world, we are diligently following the guidance and updates provided by the Centers for Disease Control and Prevention (CDC) and the World Health Organization (WHO), and are working with local public health authorities in taking the appropriate actions to help protect our employees and community. We are continuing to monitor the spread and impact of COVID-19 and are providing real-time guidance to employees in all affected regions.

Q: What protocols are in place to address the impact of COVID-19?

A: We have advanced business continuity plans in place to minimize disruptions to our business operations, including those of our customers and partners. These plans include flexible working arrangements for our employees, a comprehensive enterprise business continuity and disaster recovery program, and a global support organization with 24/7 geo-diverse team.

Q: What are some specific measures you are taking to support staff during this time?

A: In line with guidance and updates provided by the Centers for Disease Control and Prevention (CDC) and the World Health Organization (WHO), as well as our work with local public health authorities, we are enacting measures broadly and in local markets to provide the necessary support for our colleagues. We continue to provide real-time guidance for employees and take appropriate actions in accordance with the relevant public health guidance.

For example, to protect the health of our employees, we’ve reduced campus activity in the Puget Sound and the Bay Area regions and have asked employees who can work remotely to do so. However, we understand that not everyone has a role that can be done from home — so we have also decided to continue to pay all our vendor hourly service providers their regular pay during this period of reduced service needs. This is independent of whether their full services are needed. This will ensure that, in Puget Sound for example, the 4,500 hourly employees who work in our facilities will continue to receive their regular wages even if their work hours are reduced.

Q: What is Microsoft doing to help communities impacted by COVID-19?

A: Microsoft has partnered with The Seattle Foundation, United Way King County, and other companies to launch the COVID-19 Response Fund. The fund will support community organizations and nonprofits that are on the frontlines of the response. This support will include rental assistance to keep people housed; help ensure children, seniors and families have access to food; and support healthcare workers on the front line.

We are also making an initial $1 million anchor donation to help launch this effort immediately. We are encouraging and facilitate employee donations to support these efforts, which will be matched by the company on a dollar-per-dollar basis.

Q: What is Microsoft’s response now that multiple employees have tested positive for COVID-19?

A: The health and safety of our employees is a top priority at Microsoft. We are providing real-time guidance to employees in all affected regions. We will continue to monitor the situation and take action as necessary to help protect employees.

For those impacted employees, we are diligently following the guidance and updates provided by the Centers for Disease Control and Prevention (CDC) and the World Health Organization (WHO), and we are working closely with local public health authorities to provide the necessary support for those colleagues in an effort to minimize any further impact to our employees, customers, and broader community. We will continue to provide real-time guidance for employees and take appropriate actions in accordance with public health guidance.

Q: What changes to support should customers expect during COVID-19?

A: Customers should not expect any changes. We have a business continuity plan in place to provide the same level of support during this period. Our plan is currently active in countries most affected by COVID-19, and so far, we are seeing minimal impact to our operational capacity and our customer experience.

2. Business continuity

Q: How is CSS ensuring business continuity for support?

A: For impacted countries, we are rapidly enabling work from home and remote login capabilities for our advocates/support engineers and delivery partners so that they can continue to provide support for our customers. We have also set up daily syncs with our global CSS site leaders to troubleshoot support delivery issues and have backup plans in place for each of our lines of business in case the COVID-19 pandemic spreads.

Q: How are you ensuring that business isn’t disrupted across your products and services?

A: Microsoft has a comprehensive service continuity plan to keep our services running smoothly during events such as COVID-19. The plan accounts for increased usage, the ability to remotely manage our services and a geographically diverse engineering workforce to support those services.

Q: Does Microsoft have a Business Continuity Plan, and does it include Pandemic impact?

A: Microsoft has a global, all-hazards approach to its business continuity and crisis management planning. This includes a Pandemic Response Plan that connects to the enterprise response hierarchy.

Q: Is working remotely part of Microsoft’s response strategy?

A: Microsoft’s continuity plan comprises multiple impact scenarios and response strategies that provides remote capabilities.

Q: How often are Microsoft’s business continuity plans validated?

A: Microsoft tests and validates continuity plans based on criticality rating. All plans are required to validate at the level that is relevant to their criticality. All tests must be conducted within 12 months from the previous test or validation.

Q: Does Microsoft share copies of its Business Continuity Plans?

A: Microsoft does not share Business Continuity Plans externally.

Q: Are contingency plans in place to ensure all committed support SLAs and recovery time objectives (RTOs) are met?

A: Microsoft utilizes cloud-based call routing technologies which are designed to be highly resilient and route customer calls to alternate locations automatically if any location becomes unavailable ensuring seamless transition of support. Critical support infrastructure is hosted across geo-diverse data centers with load balancing or automatic failover capability.

Q: In terms of the software services provided to me as a partner, does Microsoft consider COVID-19 a “pandemic” and a “force majeure” event?

A: (For partners only) Currently, we think any such discussions are premature. We are closely monitoring the recent developments regarding the spread of the novel coronavirus (COVID-19), and our priority is the safety of our personnel, customers, partners, and community. We will update you as things develop.

3. Datacenter operations and supply chain

Q: What is the potential for service impacts? What response plans are in place if there is a regional Azure outage? And how will Microsoft communicate to customers and partners any issue or concerns related to continuity?

A: There is no expected impact to Azure services. At this time, all of our datacenters are operating normally. In the unlikely event of an outage, we have standard operating procedures for how we manage service impacting events. There is nothing preventing us from executing against these. We are monitoring the situation closely and taking extra precautions to protect the health of our employees and ensure business continuity.

If any cloud services experience a service incident such as an outage, we follow our standard, well-established communications practices to inform impacted customers and partners. This is mainly achieved through the Service Health experience in the Azure portal and/or in the M365 admin center, depending on which services are impacted.

Q: Will the COVID-19 public health situation in China impact datacenter supply chains, might that cause Azure capacity constraints?

A: We plan for contingencies including upstream supply chain interruptions, so we are actively working with our supplier base to assess the impact, and to prioritize and expedite available supply where possible to manage and minimize the impact on Azure. We are reviewing the impact of these supply chain factors on our infrastructure capacity.

In the rare situations when we do identify capacity constraints for a given region, our first order of priority is always to protect the interests of existing customers and allow them to use Azure the way they need to – including the ability to grow. Many customers take advantage of Azure so that their applications can expand/contract to respond to demand, so ensuring this ‘breathing room’ for existing solutions takes precedence over new deployments. In these situations, we work with customers to identify alternative regions and/or alternative resource types.

Q: Will Microsoft’s global infrastructure be able to keep up with demand, as organizations around the world encourage remote work?

A: We are aware of several large-scale plans by corporations, schools, and even governments to encourage remote work. In line with these plans, we have seen some spikes in usage, but these have been well-within the range of our expected usage volatility.

We continue to monitor usage very closely and are refreshing our demand model assumptions regularly to provide early visibility into any required shifts in planned capacity. As customer plans are fluid, we are proactively working to ensure we are well-positioned to provide the needed support.

- Our comprehensive service continuity plan aims to ensure that all our services keep running smoothly, even during events such as COVID-19. The plan accounts for increased usage, enables us to remotely manage our services, and leverages a geographically diverse engineering workforce to support those services.

Q: What advice is Microsoft giving CISO’s to help protect their organizations from potential cyberattacks as more organizations move to a remote work environment as they address growing health concerns?

A: CISOs and Sec admins need to look urgently at new scenarios and new threat vectors as their organizations become a distributed organization overnight, with less time to make detailed plans or run pilots. Based on our experience we recommend:

  • Enabling official chat tools to help employees know where to congregate for work.
  • Taking advantage of the six months of free premium Microsoft Teams or the removed limits on how many users can join a team or schedule video calls using the freemium version (You can follow the steps found on the supporting remote work with Teams web page).
  • Using the Open for Business Hub lists tools from various vendors that are free to small businesses during the outbreak.
  • Leverage Azure Active Directory Azure Active Directory and set up single-sign-on. This helps protect against downloading malicious links that often get passed along through emails. And, Azure AD Conditional Access helps protecting those sign-ins with Secure Defaults.
  • And, to remember to look at any policies you have set already, to make sure they don't block access for users working from home. For secure collaboration with partners and suppliers, look at Azure AD B2B.

Q: Some employees have work assigned laptops, while others use their personal devices to access company data. How can my organization help ensure our network is protected when there are disparate and unassigned personal devices being used for work purposes?

A: While it’s not uncommon in today’s work environment for employees to have work laptops they use at home; it’s likely organizations will see an increase in the use of personal devices accessing company data during this time.

Using Azure AD Conditional Access and Microsoft Intune app protection policies together helps manage and secure corporate data in approved apps on these personal devices, so employees can remain productive.

Intune, automatically discovers new devices as users connect with them, prompting them to register the device and sign in with their company credentials.

It’s important to remember that employees need a basic understanding of conditional access policies and what their devices need to connect to the corporate network, like up-to-date anti-malware protection. This way employees understand if their access is blocked and how to get the support they need.

Azure AD Application Proxy publishes on-premises apps for remote availability and if you use a managed gateway today we support several partner solutions with Secure Hybrid Access for Azure AD.

Q: My biggest concern is the short runway we have to get these practices, policies, and tools in place. What can I realistically do now?

A: The single best thing you can do to improve security for employees working from home is to turn on MFA. As you probably can’t distribute hardware security devices, use Windows Hello biometrics and smartphone authentication apps like Microsoft Authenticator.

Longer term, we recommend sec admins consider a program to find and label the most critical data, like Azure Information Protection, so you can track and audit usage when employees work from home. We must not assume that all networks are secure, or that all employees are in fact, working from home when working remotely.

Also, track your Microsoft Secure Score to see how remote working affects your compliance and risk surface. Use Microsoft Defender ATP to look for attackers masquerading as employees working from home but be aware that access policies looking for changes in user routines may flag legitimate logons from home and coffee shops.

Q: What about guidance for my employees?

A: Supporting employees will require more than just providing tools and enforcing policies. It will be a combination of tools, transparency, and timeliness.

Warn your employees to expect more phishing attempts, including targeted spear phishing aimed at high profile credentials. Now is a good time to be diligent so watch out for urgent requests that break company policy, use emotive language, and have details that are slightly wrong, and provide guidance on where to report those suspicious messages.

Establishing a clear communications policy, helps employees recognize official messages. For example, video is harder to spoof than email: an official channel like Microsoft Stream could reduce the chance of phishing while making people feel connected. Streaming videos they can view at a convenient time will also help employees juggling personal responsibilities like school closures, or travel schedule changes.

Employee trust is built on transparency. By providing clear and basic information, including how to protect their devices, will help you and employees stay ahead of threats.

Q: How can you assure Teams reliability at this time?

A: You and your team depend on our tools to stay connected and get work done. We take that responsibility seriously, and we have a plan in place to make sure services stay up and running during impactful events like this. Our business continuity plan anticipates three types of impacts to the core aspects of the service:

  • Systems: When there’s a sudden increase in usage, like the surge we recently saw in China.
  • Location: When there’s an unexpected event that is location-specific, such as an earthquake or a powerful storm.
  • People: When there’s an event that may impact the team maintaining the system, like the COVID-19 outbreak in the Puget Sound area.

We’ve recently tested service continuity during a usage spike in China. Since January 31st, we’ve seen a 500 percent increase in Teams meetings, calling, and conferences there, and a 200 percent increase in Teams usage on mobile devices. Despite this usage increase, service has been fluid there throughout the outbreak.

Our approach to delivering a highly available and resilient service centers on the following things:

  • Active/Active design: In Microsoft 365, we are driving towards having all services architected and operated in an active/active design which increases resiliency. This means that there are always multiple instances of a service running that can respond to user requests and that they are hosted in geographically dispersed datacenters.
  • Reduce incident scope: We seek to avoid incidents in the first place, but when they do happen, we strive to limit the scope of all incidents by having multiple instances of each service partitioned off from each other.
  • Fault isolation: Just as the services are designed and operated in an active/active fashion and are partitioned off from each other to prevent a failure in one from affecting another, the code base of the service is developed using similar partitioning principles called fault isolation. continuity plan. For more information, please visit the Microsoft 365 blog.

A: Surface devices empower remote workers to participate in remote meetings as if they were there in person without relying on additional peripherals thanks to the built-in high-quality speakers, microphones, and cameras, combined with the power of touch and inking. The entire Surface portfolio offers leading versatility, performance and long battery life enabling people to work comfortably and be as productive as they would be in the office from their sofa, kitchen table, or home office. Our commitment to touch screen and inking with a Surface pen enables workers and students to collaborate and ideate on documents or in whiteboarding sessions as needed.

Q: How does Surface help IT Departments manage at home/remote working scenarios both inside and outside Microsoft?

A: All Surface devices within Microsoft are now deployed via Windows Autopilot by Microsoft’s internal IT team, Core Services Engineering and Operations (CSEO). Windows Autopilot enables a zero-touch deployment for any new Surface device in any environment, to include an employee’s home or remote work environment.

This means that users can simply unbox the device (which arrives new in its box) and through the initial setup process Windows Autopilot automatically joins them to Azure Active Directory and enrolls the device into Intune for remote cloud-based management. Autopilot streamlines the shipment of the device, straight to the end-user, without the need for multiple people to physically handle and reimage the device. Microsoft and other companies across the globe are utilizing Windows Autopilot to reduce IT complexity, reduce costs, and streamline remote device deployment through the cloud.

Q: How can I ensure my employees are secure when working remotely on Surface devices?

A: Microsoft Surface products are developed with security as a principal focus. Every layer of a Surface device, from firmware to the operating system, through to cloud management, are all serviced by Microsoft through Windows Update. When a threat is identified on a Windows 10 device, at any layer, we proactively push updates to keep customers safe. For more information about how to manage your Endpoint Devices, download the e-book: A Modern Blueprint for Endpoint Protection

Q: Is it okay to use disinfectant products on my Surface device?

A: Many common disinfectant products can safely be used on Microsoft Surface devices, namely PDI Sani-Cloth Plus, CaviWipes, Covidien Alcohol Prep Pads, Total Solutions Full Spectrum Disinfectant Wipes, Clorox Healthcare Bleach Germicidal Wipes, and any similar disinfectant wipes with Isopropyl Alcohol (IPA) solution at 70% or less. Users should avoid dousing the device with a solution, but rather use pre-moistened disinfectant wipes or apply IPA to a cloth to clean the device.

These five options can be used on Surface Go, Surface Pro, Surface Pro X, Surface Book, Surface Studio, Surface Hub, Surface Pen, Silver and Sandstone Surface Laptops, and on all colors of Alcantara fabric, keyboard, and trackpads. Only IPA can be used safely on Black and Cobalt Surface Laptops. Total Solutions Disinfectant wipe and IPA can be used safely on Surface Headphones. See our Clean and care for your Surface site for more details.

Note: Microsoft cannot make a determination of the effectiveness of a given disinfectant product in fighting pathogens like COVID-19. Please refer to your local public health authority for guidance on how to stay safe from potential infection.

Q: What steps is Microsoft taking to make our guests feel at ease during their EBC engagement?

A: Both the Redmond EBC and the IEC will offer our full catalog of services for customers as well as transportation and catering. Customer and employee safety is our top concern so the EBC has added additional processes and procedures:

Following all US federal, state, regional, and local government guidelines and implementing best practices from the CDC, WHO, and the local health department.

Increased sanitization efforts throughout the entire facility, both with regards to sanitizer strength and frequency. Sanitizing shared surfaces, door handles and communal areas continually throughout the day and in between tours. The entire facility is also being deep cleaned each week.

Modifications to food and beverage items, amenities, and items provided to guests, including removal of any hard to sanitize items that are frequently touched.

Availability of hand sanitizer in all public areas.

Protocol for sick employees to ensure everyone onsite is completely healthy each day.

Regular trainings and reminders to our teams to ensure the new standards are followed and communicated to guests.

Touchless thermometers and masks are available for guests as requested.

Coordination with vendors and suppliers, including transportation, furniture handling, translation providers, catering, and tech support, to ensure compliance with our new standards and processes.

Signage requesting refraining from handshakes and encouraging good hygiene practices such as hand washing.

Q: What changes to support should customers expect during COVID-19?

A: We have a business continuity plan in place to provide the same level of support during this period; however, due to increased volumes as more people and organizations switch to remote work, customers may experience longer than normal wait times for certain products and services.

For commercial customers: If you or your commercial customer have general questions related to CSS’s response to COVID-19 that are not addressed by this FAQ, and are not about specific support incidents, contact your area’s CSS Business Engagement Manager. They will relay the question to the right support team and get you an answer.

Q: Has the COVID-19 pandemic impacted support wait times?

A: Currently, we are seeing an increasing demand from customers who have contacted our support team to help them enable remote work. As a result, we are receiving a high volume of cases, and customers are experiencing longer than normal wait times for Skype and Teams support. If your customer has submitted a case, they will be contacted once a support engineer has been assigned.

Q: Given the influx in volume, how are support cases being prioritized?

A: Our customers’ needs are at the center of our efforts, and we are quickly activating additional measures and people so that we can continue helping organizations everywhere stay connected and productive. As we adapt to best serve all our customers, we are prioritizing first responders, health and emergency management services, and critical government infrastructure organizational use, and ensuring remote workers stay up and running with the core functionality of Skype and Teams.
