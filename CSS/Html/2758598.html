Alcohol

Advertising must comply with all applicable law regulations and other requirements, including those requirements applicable to the promotion of alcohol to the public, disclaimers, notices and warnings.

Responsible drinking messaging

  • Advertising must not glorify or incite the consumption of alcohol, and should contain proper health warnings and responsible drinking messaging, such as “drink responsibly”, “do not drink and drive” etc.
  • Targeting minors is not allowed. Advertising must not be directed toward individuals under the applicable minimum drinking age, or served in a context where it is highly likely it will be displayed to such individuals. This includes, but it is not limited to, the use of language, themes, expressions, graphic resources, audio or visual elements that may appeal to underage individuals, or using individuals who are or appear to be underage in promotional content or on the website.
Important:
 		 All required disclaimers, age gating mechanisms and responsible drinking and health warnings must be present either on the ad copy or the landing page.

Deceptive products and services

Advertising is not allowed for products and services whose purpose is to enable search users to bypass or deceive a public system, regulation, procedure or individual. This type of advertising includes:

  • Essay-writing services and prewritten essays.
  • Fake IDs: Identification that is designed to disguise the accurate age, name, or other characteristics of the holder.
  • Fake diplomas and education transcripts: Advertisements from institutions that provide fake education transcripts or diplomas or that promote web-based, unaccredited colleges that offer degrees.
  • Bypassing copyright protection: Products or services that circumvent copyright protection or products that have disabled copyright protection.
  • Evading traffic tickets: Any device or service that is designed to evade speed enforcement laws, including, but not limited to laser jammers, license plate sprays and license plate covers.
  • Hacking and cracking: Sites that provide information or services to evade or bypass security systems of any kind or illegally access or tamper with software, servers, or websites.
  • Paid to click: Sites that offer to compensate users for clicking on ads or offers, or performing web searches.
  • Cable descrambling equipment: Advertising that promotes devices for descrambling cable or satellite signals.
  • Beating drug tests: Products that facilitate or promote ways to "beat" a drug test.

Q: Under deceptive products and services, what is not included in the Beating Drugs Test policy?

A: Products for ‘body cleansing’ or ‘detoxification’ that are NOT marketed for the purposes of beating a drug test (but rather to purify the body etc) are allowed.


Q: Areas of questionable legality is a wide category. What do we mean?

A: This does cover many areas and in fact we have this in place at least externally to give us a ‘catch-all’. We can choose to not accept advertising even if it is not illegal, in fact, for any reason. We reserve the right to expand or contract what content is covered under this policy at any time.

In the US, Penny Auctions are the primary sites that are rejected under this policy area. For more information about this business model, please reference the Penny Auctions Job Aid.

Q: What about sites referencing fireworks, displays or content where fireworks are not sold online?

A: Informational sites providing descriptions of fireworks products, history or fireworks events and websites providing fireworks installation services E.g. http://melrosepyro.com are allowed as are websites selling fireworks-related merchandise such as videos, books, apparel, fireworks displays or advertising of a physical location by a store that sells fireworks products offline.


Q: What is a pyramid scheme?

A: Not all online business or money making opportunities are disallowed on Microsoft Advertising. However the below is a good way to differentiate a pyramid scheme from a valid online business opportunity. It is most probably a pyramid scheme if:

  • Returns (ensuing profit or gain) rely on adding participants to the scheme, where no feasible or actual product or service to sell exists.
  • The user pays for a low value product and through the scheme/program and is expected to benefit by receiving a product of a higher value with no further action by themselves.
  • The user must pay a subscription fee or buy a specific product to join and where the value proposition of doing so is undefined (no service provided).


Q: What about landing pages that list brick and mortar (physical location) stores that sell tobacco?

A: Directory-type ads for stores that sell tobacco are allowed. Ads for tobacco stores are not allowed to sell any tobacco products on their website, but can refer search users to their physical location. There must be no online sale - no shipping or delivery option - for tobacco store advertisers.


Q: What about accessories or non-tobacco products associated with smoking?

A: Ads for smoking accessories such as humidors or lighters are acceptable. However, the corresponding landing page and ads must not promote tobacco or electronic cigarettes.


Q: What is a Usenet Site and why don’t we allow them?

A: Usenet sites display user-generated newsgroup messages or articles that are distributed via news servers. The content distributed via this network is often unrestricted and controversial in nature, especially in binary newsgroups that are capable of distributing large amounts of data (e.g,alt.binaries.warez, alt.binaries.erotica.teen. Common characteristics of Usenet/newsgroup access providers include:

  • Offer subscription plans to access usenet/newsgroup content
  • Detail download parameters (e.g. unlimited, anonymous, secure)
  • Promote access speed, message completion rates and retention time

Microsoft Advertising does not accept advertising from such sites due to historical concerns with the types of content distributed using Usenet.


Q: What are not considered firearms?

A: In the United States and Canada markets, the following are allowed: cap guns, stun guns, water pistols, airsoft guns, nail guns, paintball guns, holsters, grips, sights/scopes, tripods, finishers. Additionally, the following are allowed:

  • BB Guns: Low power-BB guns, which are equivalent to Airsoft and are therefore acceptable. However, high-power air rifles (BB guns) are equivalent to gun powder rifles and are not allowed.
  • Paintball guns, "Airsoft" guns, replicas, and imitation or "look-alike” firearms
  • Imitation firearms. However, they must have clear markings permanently affixed to them (commonly an orange plug inserted in the barrel), and must not be readily convertible to shoot a projectile.
    • This includes replicas/imitation guns of any age, as well as toy and cap guns.

More information on enforcement of Weapons policy is available in the Weapons Job Aid.

Q: Do we allow ads for hemp oil?

A: Hemp oil falls into a highly contested legal gray area. However, it appears that hemp oil, when devoid of all cannabinoids, is permitted for sale in the United States. When hemp oil contains any THC or CBD (other than trace amounts), it is considered a controlled substance and disallowed. Analysis follows:

Hemp is a variety of cannabis sativa and is the same plant species as marijuana. "Industrial hemp," as it is commonly called, is a low-concentration THC variety of the cannabis plant intended to be used for purposes such as the production of cosmetics, paper, rope, and other goods. "Marihuana" and "tetrahydrocannabinols" are both Schedule I controlled substances in the United States. See Controlled Substances Act ("CSA") Schedule I. The CSA defines "marihuana" to include only certain portions of the plant. See 21 USC § 802(16) ("The term 'marihuana' [...] does not include the mature stalks of [the] plant, fiber produced from [the] stalks, oil or cake made from the seeds of [the] plant, any other compound, manufacture, salt, derivative, mixture, or preparation of such mature stalks [...], fiber, oil, or cake, or the sterilized seed of such plant.").

The regulation of hemp in the US has been heavily litigated; circuit courts and regulators disagree on its status. The Ninth Circuit Court of Appeals and hemp lobbyists argue that products made from industrial hemp fit entirely within the marijuana plant carve-out found in the CSA. See Hemp Industries Association v. DEA, 357 F.3d 1012, 1017 (9th Cir. 2004) ("The non-psychoactive hemp in Appellants' products is derived from the 'mature stalks' or is 'oil and cake made from the seeds' of the Cannabis plant, and therefore fits within the plainly stated exception to the CSA definition of marijuana."). The Eighth Circuit Court of Appeals argues that hemp is not exempt from control – because Cannabis sativa contains THC, and THC is separately scheduled. According to the Eighth Circuit: "The plain language of the CSA states that schedule I(c) includes ‘any material. . . which contains any quantity of THC’ and thus such material is regulated.” United States v. White Plume, 447 F.3d 1067, 1073 (8th Cir. 2006).

The DEA makes a similar argument: Any part of the marijuana plant that contains cannabinoids is controlled. See here ("Congress placed 'marihuana' in Schedule I of the CSA and defined 'marihuana' as all parts of the plant Cannabis sativa L., with certain exceptions for the parts of the plant that are not the source of cannabinoids. CBD derived from the cannabis plant is controlled under Schedule I of the CSA because it is a naturally occurring constituent of marijuana."); see also here (page 176). Recently, the DEA clarified this position:

As the scientific literature indicates, cannabinoids, such as tetrahydrocannabinols (THC), cannabinols (CBN) and cannabidiols (CBD), are found in the parts of the cannabis plant that fall within the CSA definition of marijuana, such as the flowering tops, resin, and leaves. According to the scientific literature, cannabinoids are not found in the parts of the cannabis plant that are excluded from the CSA definition of marijuana, except for trace amounts (typically, only parts per million) that may be found where small quantities of resin adhere to the surface of seeds and mature stalk. Thus, based on the scientific literature, it is not practical to produce extracts that contain more than trace amounts of cannabinoids using only the parts of the cannabis plant that are excluded from the CSA definition of marijuana, such as oil from the seeds.

See Clarification of the New Drug Code (7350) for Marijuana Extract (emphasis added).

Bing follows the DEA's interpretation: Products that contain cannabinoids (such as THC and CBD) should be considered controlled substances and disallowed.


Q: Do we allow ads for drug retreats?

A: No. Typically, these advertisers offer a stay at a "retreat center" where psychoactive substances, such as iboga, ayahuasca, or cannabis, are made available (for example, for detox or recovery). They are disallowed even if the website does not promote or ship drugs to consumers. This is not to be confused with rehab centers, which are allowed.


Q: Are crossbows and bow and arrows allowed under the weapons policy?

A: Yes, crossbows and bow and arrows are considered “sporting goods” and are not disallowed under the weapons policy. The exception is if the ad copy is marketed to use crossbows to incite violence.
