1. Symptoms

1.1 Why is this happening? 

When we see activity in the customer's account that looks like someone else might have gotten their password, we lock the account down to prevent anyone from accessing personal information. The recovery process allows account owners to get back into their account while keeping others locked out.

Customer will see this message when we have seen some activity on their account that is out of the ordinary enough for us to take measures to lock their account until they can take action. Possible causes could be excessive failed login attempts, login from unknown devices, login from unknown location, or that we suspected your account has been taken over by someone. The flag is permanent until the user takes the action.

If a customer asks how their account might have been compromised, say that we cannot tell for certain that it was compromised or how, but there are some things you can do to make your account more secure at Microsoft account security.

1.2 Things to ask

What have you tried to do so far? If the customer has already tried using security info, try again with them per section 3.1. 

If the customer has already skipped to the "Recover your Microsoft account" form, ask why and see if they can use their security info instead, per section 3.1. 

Otherwise, refer to KB 3123259 Procedure: Microsoft account: The "Recover your Microsoft account" form. 
How many times have you tried to do that? If the customer has used the account recovery form multiple times and is absolutely sure they cannot use security info, then skip to section 4.1.

2. Customer experience

2.1 Account-is-compromised walkthrough – account.microsoft.com

1. Customers are prompted to verify their account with a security code when first signing-in after an account is compromised. If they can do this without a problem, they will see the "Help us secure your account" error message after sign-in. 

Customer selects Next to continue.		 [Asset 4509072]
2. The customer is prompted to set a new password. Customer enters a new password that cannot be one they used previously and must be at least 8 characters. Customer then re-enters that same password and selects Next to continue.

Note: Some helpful hints to share about creating strong passwords:
  • Don’t re-use passwords on important accounts.
  • Create a "passphrase" instead of just one word. A small phrase like "MyDogIs5!" is sometimes easier to remember and harder to hack than a password like "r7Mq93&".
  • Mix it up with upper and lower-case letters along with numbers and even symbols.
  • Please note the 16-character max limit if you have an Xbox360. If you don’t then you can make a longer password if it’s easier for you to remember a longer one. 
 [Asset 4509073]
3. The customer is asked to review their security info. If there are any pieces of security info listed that a customer does not recognize or can no longer use, they should select the checkbox next to that item to uncheck and remove it from the list of their security info. Once finished verifying security info, select Next to continue.

Do your own quick review – lookup the account in MST and review the Microsoft Account Status section. Do you see any security info added recently? 

Note: If a customer does remove some security info, advise that they should sign in to http://account.microsoft.com after finishing this and replace removed security info with new security info, especially if they end up with less than three pieces of security info associate with their account as a result.		 [Asset 4509074]
4. This message may not appear, but if the customer has had other problems with their account, they will be advised to scan for malware and ensure that Windows update is enabled. Once the customer has finished, select Next to continue.

The links direct customers as follows:

Scan for malware- http://www.microsoft.com/security/scanner/default.aspx

Check Windows update- http://windows.microsoft.com/windows-8/windows-update-faq
 
  		 [Asset 4509075]
5. The customer will receive confirmation that their account is now unlocked and they can sign in as usual with their new password.

The customer will be referred to:

http://windows.microsoft.com/windows/outlook/hacked-account for more info about compromised accounts and can select Sign in with your new password to finish signing into what they were trying to access.

Important: Certain security settings are reset to protect a customer’s info when an account is compromised. Walk the customer through the steps in the next section to fix those settings.		 [Asset 4509076]

2.2 Account-is-compromised walkthrough - Xbox One

When attempting to sign in on an Xbox One console, a customer may receive the compromise message. The customer should select Fix this now. [Asset 4077468]
The customer is asked to verify their identity via an emailed code. [Asset 4077469]
Once the customer verifies the code received in email, they will be asked to pick a new password. [Asset 4077470]
The customer will be asked to verify the new password. [Asset 4077471]
After the new password is accepted, the customer will be asked to review their security information. [Asset 4077473]
The customer will be able to add an alternate email address if they want. [Asset 4077474]
The workflow ends by taking the customer back to the sign-in screen. [Asset 4077475]

2.3 What to do after getting into a compromised account

Customers should do the following after getting back into an account that was compromised in order to protect their information and restore settings that may have been reset to protect their account when it was compromised.

Scenario

Customer action
If apps, services, or subscriptions were purchased with this account:
  1. Sign in to Account.microsoft.com.
  2. Select Review your purchases and make sure there aren't any new transactions you did not make or expect.
  3. Select Billing info and make sure your billing information was not changed.
If customer indicates they see unauthorized charges, refer to KB 930018 Procedure: How to handle account takeover on a Microsoft account.
If OneDrive is used with this account to store and share files, photos, or other data:
  1. Sign in to OneDrive.live.com
  2. Select Shared on the side menu.
  3. Review the folders and files that you’re currently sharing to make sure you’re not sharing anything with people that you don’t know or that shares you had made weren’t deleted.
If the account is an Outlook or Hotmail email account:
  1. Sign in to outlook.com
  2. Select the [Asset 4509077] Settings icon , then Options.
  3. Check the following settings and restore any that might have been removed or changed.
    • Send/receive accounts: Managing your account > Your email accounts.
    • Reply-to address: Writing email > Reply-to address.
    • Email forwarding: Managing your account > Email forwarding.
    • Auto-reply: Managing your account > Sending automated vacation replies.
  4. Check your Deleted mail folder and your Contacts to make sure none of your contacts or messages were deleted.

The best way for the customer to get back into an account is to use security info. While there are other options, we should do everything we can to use security info first.

3.2 Use a recovery code

If all attempts to use a security code have been exhausted, check if the customer has previously printed out a recovery code. Remember, it’s rare that customers record that code, so this option is very uncommon. 

Ask:

Did you ever create and print out a recovery code?
 * If No, skip to "Use Account Recovery" section.
If Yes, have the customer select I can't verify my account.

[Asset 4509078]

The option to use a recovery code will appear if one was created. Once it appears, enter the code and select Use recovery code.

[Asset 4509079]

If successful, the customer should receive a prompt that their account was compromised and can proceed from the steps outlined in Section 2.1. 

If not, refer to KB 3123259 Procedure: Microsoft account: The "Recover your Microsoft account" form.

4. Engagement with Microsoft-based Advanced Capability Advocates

What to do if you cannot resolve this?

If you tried all of the steps above AND the customer has tried to use the "Recover your Microsoft account" form multiple times without success:

Ask customer if they have an Xbox they sign into regularly with this account:

1. If yes, follow the scenario types below:

  • Has any of the following occurred:
Customer reported unknown charges from Microsoft
Customer reported a different Microsoft account sign-in address than what the account is currently set as
Customer does not recognize any of the security info associated with the account
  • Refer to section 2.1 Account takeover of KB 817557 Guidelines: Advanced Capability Advocate engagement.

2. If no, refer to KB 3107652 Procedure: Microsoft account: Customer cannot regain access to account for next steps.
