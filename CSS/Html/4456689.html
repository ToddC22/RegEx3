Procedure: Scoping

[Asset 4462485]

Who should use this article?
Business Process Manager (CCE), Escalation Engineer (CSS), Support Engineer (CSS)

What is this article about?
This procedure covers how to work with a Customer or Partner to ensure the impact of their issue is understood, clearly identify what will be looked at for their Service Request and reach agreement on what the desired end state outcome at closure will be.

Overview

Note: For full guidance on delivering a high-quality Initial response, combined with scoping at the start of a support case, this article needs to be read along side the 4464166 | Guidance: First Quality Response (FQR) and 4456925 | Procedure Initial Response articles.

Scoping must be done before any technical troubleshooting or log collection is performed. You need to include scoping in your initial contact with the customer. Follow guidance in 4464166 | Guideline: First Quality Response (FQR), to demonstrate your understanding of the issue and to progress the case.

As appropriate, each technology may provide guidance on the depth of scoping needed and the degree a written, electronic agreement on scope is required before troubleshooting. For example, a cloud outage may require a very limited verbal scope while an Azure Dev team may require a more complete scope.

Cases must be re-scoped with the Customer or Partner if the initially agreed scope is found to be inaccurate or the closure conditions need to be changed.

When scoping a case

  1. Confirm the following:
    • Primary and secondary (if available) contact details and preferred contact method.
    • Impact
    • Severity (agree a severity that is appropriate and reflects the impact)
      For Sev B cases, confirm if the Customer or Partner wants 24x7 support and will be available 24x7
    • Environment information
    • Technical detail
    • Closure expectations
  2. For Partner 'Cloud Solution Provider (CSP)' cases make sure you:
    • Understand whether the Partner is acting on behalf of their end customer.
    • Understand what specific solution they are working on with the customer.
    • Verify what specific help is needed from Microsoft.
    • Confirm what diagnostics and troubleshooting have been completed and what the results were.
  3. If there are any likely concerns such as Third-Party issues or unsupported scenarios raise them with the Customer or Partner:
    • See the Third-Party issues procedure for additional information.
  4. Set callback expectations for how regularly you will communicate throughout the case.
  5. Check the case coding and incident type is correct and update if required:
    • If the incident is not break-fix follow the Advisory or Root Cause Analysis procedures.
  6. Make sure that case title accurately reflects the technical issue.
  7. Check the following are set or added correctly:
    • Routing product.
    • Support Topic down to the lowest level possible.

Documenting and sending the scoping email

  1. Send the email as soon as possible after the scoping conversation:
  2. Write the scope in your own words:
    • Do not copy and paste any information from the case description or Customer or Partner emails into the scope.
    • Include as much technical detail as possible.
    • Describe the business impact in nontechnical words.
    • Describe specific closure conditions without repeating the issue definition.
    • The scoping email must capture all information discussed from the scoping call. Leave nothing out, the more detailed the scope the better.
    • The Customer or Partner should be able to see that we have understood all the information that was discussed.
  3. For Break-fix cases (where something is broken, and the expectation is that it be fixed), AVOID using the words like "root", "cause", "why", "how" in the agreed resolution.
    • We may frequently find cause as part of our troubleshooting, but we do not commit to it in the resolution statement of a break-fix case.
  4. For global English reasons keep your scope direct and simple:
    • Keep the sentences short.
    • Use simple subject-verb construction instead of complex constructions.
  5. If the customer does not agree to the scope, re-scope the issue with customer to gain agreement:
    • For Premier or Unified Support customers, engage the Incident Manager (IM) as necessary.
  6. Once scoping has been completed CAP users must set the Disposition to 'Scope Confirmed'.

Frequently asked questions

Q: What are the benefits of good scoping?

 

  • Increases customer/partner satisfaction
  • Inspires customer/partner confidence
  • Establishes necessary boundaries, priorities and sets the case expectations
  • Ensures the first action plan is technically valid
  • Helps if there is a case transfer
  • Reduces case labor
  • Ensures customers/partners are billed correctly for advisory vs. break/fix work
  • Creating a clear and common understanding of the technical problem
  • Agreeing with the customer/partner the conditions that have to be met before case closure
  • Defining what is/isn’t supported by Microsoft
  • Ensuring the prioritization of the case matches the customer's/partner's expectations based on the impact on their business
  • To ensure only one issue is covered per case

Q: Can I include troubleshooting instructions/questions or suggested solutions in my scoping email?

A: Yes, as part of the First Quality Response behaviour, it is expected that the initial response, scoping and troubleshooting activities are brought together at the beginning of the case to improve the customer experience.

Where there is any ambiguity to the customer’s expectations it is still very important to complete the scoping flow to have a shared understanding of the customer issue and expectations.

Q: Can I rescope a case at any time?

A: Yes, update any scoping fields in the case as appropriate.
