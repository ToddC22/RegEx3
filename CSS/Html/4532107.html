Important: Use these steps as a guide not a script.

2. Guiding principles

The talking points in this article are intended to demonstrate the techniques explained under each step of the process. You can use it when applicable, or can adapt them according to the customer’s profile, scenario, market, culture, and language supported. Each interaction is unique, it’s important to focus on the customer’s need and pace making sure that in the end all interaction steps are considered.

Use the product conversation as an example, but do not replace the product specific knowledge resources that represent the single source of truth in terms of product features, differentiators, pitch, or scenarios.

Note: The following steps apply to all contact types (post and pre-sales).

The opening sets the pace of the conversation and starts building a connection with your customer. For additional sales specific greetings guidance, review the table below:

3.1  Greet the customer and confirm the request

The way you open the conversation sets the pace for the rest of the interaction.

Be engaging, use an opening that signals you’re different, and what they’re going to experience with you is more than just a normal support interaction.

Emotionally connected customers buy more, visit more often, exhibit less price sensitivity, pay more attention to your communications, follow your advice, and recommend your brand.

Greetings are best kept casual – Don’t be too formal, and introduce yourself in the greeting.

Bear in mind the messages already provided by the Microsoft Store bot, including branding (there is no need to use more).

Keep your welcome clean and simple.

Reply to the first message as soon as possible, prospective customers typically become impatient if they don’t receive an answer right away.

Open the call with a smile – A smile can be heard, and a smiling voice is welcoming and relaxing. The customer will subconsciously appreciate it.

“Good morning/good afternoon/Hello/Good day…”

“…. Thank you for calling Microsoft Store Support, my name is <name>,…”

Start the conversation with a “Warm Up” – With a simple question such as ‘how is your day so far?’, most people will respond in a friendly manner and it helps to break the ice.

“… How can I help you achieve more today”

“… and my favorite Microsoft product is <product name>. Tell me a little about yourself and the reason for calling today.”

“…We are having a wonderful day at Microsoft, how is your day going today?”

“…How can I empower you today?”

“…How is your Friday going?”

“How may I be of service?”

“What can I assist you with?”

3.2 Ask for their name

The best place to ask for the customer’s name is during the opening. Customers will be more open to provide their name if a personal connection is created from the beginning. When you introduce yourself with your name, the use of personal pronoun (you, my, I) will show personal involvement and will create a connection.

“Who do I have the pleasure of speaking with today?”

“I am happy/definitely I can help you with that/sorry to hear that, will you please tell me your first name?”

4. Identify the customer’s needs

Identify and discover the customer’s true need by using various questioning techniques to gather the right information. This helps move to the next phase and reach a resolution without needing ask additional questions.

Understanding the customer’s needs helps provide the right solution, prevent customer’s objections, reduces the overall conversation, prevents further contacts, and increases the customer’s satisfaction.

Messaging specifics

Tips and techniques

4.1 Frame the need

Frame questions in a way that reflects a genuine desire to help.

Before asking the customer additional questions, show that you are listening to the customer's first words and are committed to help.

Rephrase and restate what you understand the customer is requesting to make sure you are addressing the right topic.

Do not simply restate the customer’s words, attempt to rephrase demonstrating that you are committed to listening and understanding.

Mirror the customer's level of technical expertise (for example, guiding them faster or slower, through steps).

Framing the customer’s needs is a mandatory step. It confirms the customer’s questions and defines the scope of what you are addressing.

Allow customers to explain themselves.

Give the customers the opportunity to fully outline their need without interruption.

Engage: “I hear that ABC is an issue for you, is that right?”

Mirror the customer’s language to confirm the concern:

Frame: “It sounds like what you may have here is a case of …”

“Tell me what you are looking to do...”

4.2 Ask questions

Don’t be afraid to dig a little deeper to help uncover the full story of their situation.

However, don’t expect immediate or detailed answers, unless you’ve established some form of trust or connection.

All three question types 1. Open, 2. Clarifying and probing, and 3. Closed should be leveraged though not always in the same order. The customer responses and flow of the conversation should guide the order of the questions.

  • Open ended questions helps discover the level of customer’s interest, on which you need to build further.

Clarifying questions are looking for more facts and the answers are typically brief, factual, short, and specific.

Share any available insights that you have at hand to open the conversation.

  • Rephrase and summarize the information gathered from the customer.
  • Highlight the newly identified need by confirming correct understanding and alignment with the customer’s perspective.

Set expectations with the customer that you’re happy to help but need context to do so. Customers will be responsive if they are aware of why you’re asking for more details.

  • Keep your questions short, simple, and packed. Do not split your message into multiple lines.

Use additional details to understand if the solution in question is the best one for the customer’s needs, and recommend follow-up actions (for example, callback).

  • Do not say "No" or "I can’t help you". If their request is not available or is unclear, ask questions to gather more information and provide a helpful response.

Make sure your communication is simple and concise, let the customer answer before sending additional questions.

How, what, when, where and who questions can be used as open questions or clarifying questions.

Why should be asked respectfully as it may make the customer feel they have to justify themselves. It can also feel aggressive.

  • Too many how, what, when and where questions can cause customers to feel as if they are being interrogated.

Examples of open questions:

“Please tell me what happened when you tried to add your card in your account?”

“What activities will you use the device for?”

Probing questions are open questions beginning with what, why or how, the answers allow customers to provide additional insights.

Examples of probing questions:

“What can you say about the battery life of your previous Surface?”

“How did you find the number of ports on our Surface Pro 7?”

Questions can be softened by including tell, explain, and describe (TED) questions.

“Tell me more about that.”

“Explain the situation to me.”

“Describe that to me.”

Listen:

“That’s interesting”

“Tell me more”

“What's behind that?”

Rephrase, summarize, and check understanding:

"What I'm hearing is..."

"Sounds like you are saying... “

"What do you mean when you say."

"Is this what you mean? “

“Let me rephrase the question…”

“If I understand correctly, you would like to…”

4.3 Uncover the real need

Customers might ask about a specific Office version or a specific Surface configuration.

It’s recommended that you investigate further to understand what the customer’s intentions are with the product or service. This will allow you to help the customer confirm their initial goal or enable you to suggest alternate options that may work better.

  • Probing questions help determine the needs of the customer.

Always go through the discovery phase to make sure the customer’s needs are clear and you uncovered it with a simple question.

To expend on a response, the use of Exactly and Can you explain further, should get you there.

4.4 Use systems and data

Use the available tools to get additional context and to be able to deliver a personalized support.

Share the case ID for further reference.

Use context details on LiveEngage. Use SR creation whenever a follow-up action is needed for future advocates and the customer’s benefit.

“I see that this is not the first time you are contacting us for this issue, have you contacted your bank meanwhile?”

“I see you are contacting us from Canada, and you are attempting to make a purchase from a US website”

“As per the notes taken by my colleague last week you were interested in purchasing a Surface Laptop but you needed more time to think about it. Have you made a decision yet?”

4.5 Account verification/fortify

Reviewing the customer’s information can save time and customer effort by checking:

  • The account status.
  • Previously purchased products.
  • Order attempts.
  • Various blockers.
  • Previous contact history with support.

While you might think there is an effort for the customer to provide their Microsoft account information, or to receive and communicate the security code, in fact, checking your systems first may be lower effort than asking a lot of questions or having the customer attempt to explain details that you would immediately see by yourself in the systems.

While account verification is preferred, it may not be necessary.

Verify the account for support scenarios where system data can add additional context to the conversation (for example, purchase history, purchase attempts, and subscription version).

See KB 4506610 Procedure: Microsoft account verification

“We just need to go through a short verification process. On the email address associated with the purchase I will send you an email with a support code. Please check your inbox and provide me with the support code.”

“We will go through a verification process. I’ll send you a code to your email address, please provide it to me once you have received it.”

“Before I'm able to move forward, I will need to send a code via SMS to verify your Microsoft account. Please provide me the code once you received it.”

“For me to verify the account, I will be needing to send a code through your email, once received please share it with me. Let me go ahead and send now.”

5. Solution discovery/develop solution

Creating a solution means building a valued proposition by using the information gathered, and matching the right opportunity based on the customer’s needs. The product or service offered is only excellent if it fulfils the customer’s needs. Answering to the customer immediate question without understanding the underlying need and reason for making that specific request is a behavior related to transactional approach, therefore, as a trusted advisor driving consultative conversations always make sure that the real need is being surfaced and the solution provided addresses it.

Checkpoint details

Tips and techniques

5.1 Match opportunity and add value

The sales advocate is a valued trusted advisor and problem-solver, rather than someone merely promoting a product or service. Sales advocates, they need to ensure that the customer feels satisfied with the whole purchase experience.

Consultative selling is about the dialogue between the advocate and the customer, it’s about relationship building and listening first, and then tailoring the solution accordingly, that will leverage the existing opportunities.

A trusted advisor is selling solutions not products. A product or service is only excellent if it fulfils the customer’s needs and adds value.

Use online resources such as links for further reference whenever available.

  • Present the option for POBO and ask for the sale.

5.2 Right solution and option

Consider the customer’s pain points

The better you understand the customer’s situation, the better you can serve their needs. A call may be regarding a simple issue but after understanding the need, a tablet may be a more suitable option.

Highlight the differences

Be able to position the value of the product against competitors and demonstrate that the presented solution is the best fit for their needs.

Build valued proposition

When presenting a solution, include concrete information that the customer will appreciate under the key areas below:

  • Improve quality
  • Reduce expenses
  • Increase productivity
  • Mitigate a risk
  • Fully understand the customer’s need before jumping to a solution.
  • Follow the solution guidelines sub steps, making sure that your messages are simple, clear, and related to the solution you are offering to the customer.
  • Limit each chat message to five sentences or less. Prospective customers starts a live chat conversation because they want their questions answered quickly and efficiently. Keep your responses brief, or they may become frustrated.

5.3 Use promos or offers when applicable

Be aware of all promotions available for the market you are supporting, know your store website and make sure you are highlighting promos applicable to the customer’s situation. Quote the price and emphasize the discount.

Use the Promo SharePoint for an overview of current and upcoming promotions.

5.4 Alternatives

What to say if the customer is asking for something we don’t have or can’t do?

For example, a product is out of stock, not released in that market, the discount or promotion is not available anymore, or promotions and other discounts are not stackable.

Suggest viable alternatives rather than saying ‘no’.

If the customer asks for an offer or discount that is not available offer the next best thing.

By doing a need discovery phase you will know what the customer wants to achieve and would will be able to offer an alternative solution and focus the discussion on the value of what you are suggesting to the customer rather than the price or the unavailable option.

Reframe a No using positive language:

“I can see why you would ask for this item/promo/bundle, but I’m afraid that item is out of stock right now/promotion has expired. I can suggest a similar item/offer we do have, let’s discuss about what are you having in mind to achieve with a…”

5.5 Compare

Avoid focusing on a single solution. Use what is known about the customer’s situation to show how two or three products or services can be solutions.

Comparisons are more than reading a list of specs and differences between products available to customers.

Customers need options. To provide that freedom of choice, you can offer a variety of products, subscriptions, and payment options.

Use available online resources like Help me choose, compare devices or compare Windows 10 Home vs. Pro.

5.6 Recommend

Provide your expert advice on what products best suit the customer’s needs.

“Based on your requirements I highly recommend the Surface Book 2 as that device is advisable for intense gaming and running professional software like AUTO cad and photoshopping.”

“You could try for that the Surface Pro 6 for gaming and optimal performance I could recommend one with 16GB RAM, 512 GB SSD and intel I7 processor.”

“You said you are using multiple devices; therefore, I suggest/recommend you pick he Office 365 Home that allows you multiple installations and seamless experience across the devices.”

“As your son wants to play Minecraft with his friends, this Xbox LIVE membership will be key to ensuring he’ll be able to play multiplayer games with everyone.”

“You mentioned that your employees need to have access to essential software. As a small business, this Office 365 subscription is the best fit for gearing up your team to do their best work.”

"We talked about using Outlook heavily earlier, so I'd recommend Office 365 Home, so you get all of the Office apps you need as well as 1TB of OneDrive storage."

5.7 Romance the product

Romancing is using persuasive arguments to get your customer’s attention. Build interest and excitement about your product using short, repeatable wording that conveys the essential elements of the product.

Romancing is also using key moments/promo beats messaging aligned with campaign story and messaging.

  • Share the product story.
  • Highlight key differentiators that define the product or product family experience.
  • Explain why customers love that product.
  • Use the product pitch.
  • Adopt the product marketing language.
  • Adopt the campaign marketing language and messaging to pitch a promotion or a key moment.
  • Tell your personal story – Create excitement by being excited about the product.
  • Know the product very well.
  • Be happy and smiling while talking about the product throughout the entire interaction.

Enthusiasm in messaging means showing that you are a knowledgeable fan of the product that you support.

You can connect the customer’s needs with various persona, or with their own experiences with the product.

  • Note: Keep the communication clean and aligned with the messaging guidelines.

Use the product article, the product Battle Cards and PDPs for the key product pitch and why customers love it and why they buy it.

5.8 Quote price or promo

Explicitly mention applicable price and discount offers when applicable. This is powerful when customers are fully convinced about the value of the solution you are offering. It can enable customers to achieve more by using that solution.

Whenever you feel the price discussion comes up too early in the conversation, deflect back to the current discussion.

Alternately quickly mention the price of a configuration and then move the conversation back to something related to the value.

Position the price considering the key value drivers tailored to customer’s needs: “You talked about the need to …. Based on that, you’d be best suited for our … bundle because it includes …, …, and …”

Avoiding a too early price discussion: “There are many options, and I need to understand your needs before I can give you an accurate quote.”

Price and question: "The price is… and that includes…. What criteria, other than price, will you be using to make your final decision?"

Price and Benefit: "The price is $.... for the …. option. What this means to you is that you’ll have …. How important is this benefit to you?"

Price and personalization: "The price is $.... for our…. package. I’m thinking this is the right package for you because you …. Let me ask you some additional questions to confirm that I’m on the right track."

Price and urgency: "The price is $.... and we can honor this special offer for 48 hours. I’m mentioning this because it sounds like price is important to you. What will the impact of this cost saving opportunity be on your decision-making process?"

Price and budget check: "The preliminary price is $.... Tell me how this sounds in terms of your budget/the prices you’ve heard from others."

5.9 Ask for the sale

You build a report, asked all the necessary discovery questions to understand what the customer wants to achieve, learned about the customer's concerns, answered all questions, and presented a competitive solution where the customer sees the value. You did it all right, and now closing the sales is a natural part in of your conversation.

Look for buying signals and doing a pre-closing check.

Ask the question

Let the customer know that you can make complete the purchase. Offer to sell it right now and ask the customer to make a decision by offering to close the sale during the interaction. Make sure you create awareness about the possibility to make the order with a purchase on behalf of for the customer (POBO) as soon as possible. Some customers love to just talk. If you see they like it, get into the game, but and keep coming back to your POBO offer. Always let customers know that we can help.

Create urgency and follow-up

If the customer needs additional time to think about it, always agree on a call (increase your chances on closing follow-up the sale).

Make sure that you present the option for POBO and ask for the sale.

If they are not ready to commit, provide them with additional information or follow up details.

“I’m really happy I could answer your questions. Let me share with you a couple of additional resources.”

Look for buying signals during the conversation:

  • The customer agrees with most of the points you make.
  • The customer is responsive.
  • The customer uses upbeat and positive.
  • The customer asks specific questions.
  • The customer seeks clarification.

You can also encourage this by checking the customer’s understanding and comfort throughout the sale.

Pre-closing check

If the discussion is going in the right direction, you can ask customers if they are happy and have understood everything, they have heard so far. This provides an indication of how the customer is feeling about the purchase and potentially how to approach the closing.

“Are you happy with what you have heard so far?”

“Do you like what you have heard so far?”

“Do you feel comfortable with everything that I’ve said?”

You just need to ask the question. Being bold and asking for the sale will get a yes more often than if you never asked. Have favorite close phrases prepared and use them. Here are some examples:

“In your opinion, does what I am offering address your need of….”

“Are you happy that this would do the job for you? Ideally when do you want to take delivery of this?”

Discover if the customer is sold on the product. If the answer is no, seek to understand and manage the customer’s concerns and objections.

“From what you’ve told me and what we’ve discussed this seems to have the lot. Would you like to go ahead with it?”

“So, this seems like the perfect package for you. The next step is to get it ordered. Can we proceed?

“So, do you get the blue one or the grey one?”

“Could we start building the cart for you?”

“I think that's everything, isn't it really? Should we go ahead then?”

“It seems like we've giving you all you are looking for based on our earlier discussion. Can we start the order and wrap things up?”

This technique works well when there is a good rapport with the customer.

“From what you’ve told me, you need a ... and a ...Given that this model has everything you need, would you like to go ahead today?”

The advocate summarizes the discussion so far to validate that the customer’s needs have been addressed and to confirm they wish to move forward.

“How do you feel about what we have discussed so far?”

“What direction do you want to go from here?”

A soft approach that allows the customer to determine what to do next, without any pressure or persuasion.

When purchasing a gift for somebody else:

“Do you think they would like these?”

“What color would they like it in?”

“Your daughter is going to love using this laptop.”

Create a sense of urgency and establish value before offering a discount or promotion.

“This is the last day of this promotion”

“We got a x% discount for customers who purchase this configuration by…”

Follow-up

Yes, it can happen. Some customers might not have decided at this point. In this scenario, the most important thing is to keep be ready to take the next step and leverage the sale with you.

Strike 1: You need to take an extra step with you:

“Ok, that's fine, should I phone you in a week or two days?”

“How long it will take to check?”

“Should I call you back on Thursday?”

Second contact: If the customer says that they still does not have the money:

“When do you think you are having it?”

“Should I phone you back beginning of next month?”

Success: If they say yes, this is great because you are still in the sales process and already agreed to the next action that is a 100% in your control.

Third contact: You are offering to do the customer a favor. When you call back you would say:

“I am phoning you as arranged/as promised.”

Important: Always call when a follow up was agreed to, keep your promise, stick to it.

5.10 Overcome objections

No matter how good we are at listening, clarifying, summarizing and precision questioning, the customer will come up with some objections, or barriers.

An objection can be a sign of concern or fear on the buyer’s part. The logical reasons are that they haven’t yet received a compelling enough reason to make the purchase (the customer isn’t convinced), haven’t had all their needs or concerns addressed, or the customer doesn’t understand parts of the interaction.

They may also not trust you yet or don’t believe you, or that your product can help them.

The customer might also attempt to hide the reason for their objections.

Alternatively, an objection can also be a good sign. It could mean that the customer is engaged and actively considering the purchase. Sometimes the objection is a request for more information or for additional confirmation. The fact that customers are raising their concerns, gives you the opportunity to address it.

Psychological reasons for objections:

  • Dislike decision making.
  • Prefers old habits.
  • Reluctance to give up something old for something new.
  • Unpleasant previous experience.

Note: Objections might occur in any phase of the conversation but most probably during the Ask for the sale phase. It can also come up on a non-sales interaction if the customer is not immediately happy with the solution (delivery of hard messages).

Acknowledge the customer’s concerns or objections, so they feel they are being heard. Remember to pause and listen. This does not mean agree with the customer.

"I appreciate that…"

“I can imagine how frustrating that is.”

“I can appreciate how you must feel.”

“This is a significant purchase decision; you probably want to make sure you are making the right decision?”

“It sounds like we’ve found the perfect Surface for you and you just want to make sure you are getting a good deal”

Clarify the exact concern and get to the root cause. Align yourself with the customer’s concern.

"Which aspects of…?"

“Tell me more…”

“Is that so?”

“So, what I am hearing you say is…”

“If I understand correctly, you…”

Qualify the scope of the concern so you can narrow it down.

"Without that concern, would you…"

Empathize

Show the customer not only that you understand, but you care. Sometimes is ok to articulate something that relates you to the customer’s situation.

“Feel, Felt, Found "

“I understand why you feel that way and I would have the same concern”

“I’ve spoken to other customers who also felt this way…”

“What they found was that even though comparative products may be cheaper, they lack some of the core features”

“I can see how you'd feel this way. I felt the price was a little high, but I found it's a real value because of all the built-in software and capabilities.”

Respond

Don’t jump to conclusions instead suggest next steps. Ask for feedback on your suggestion or ask them for a suggestion to move the conversation. Identify solutions together by involving the customer. Gain a clear, mutual agreement on a solution, then act on the solution.

“May I suggest that… "

“Would you agree…”

“Would it be helpful to share some of the things I’ve learned working with other customers in similar situations that might be helpful?”

“I believe this solution is the best, because, as you stated before, battery life is very important to you…”

5.11 Show empowerment

Customers appreciate when you focus on what you can do rather than what you can’t do.

There might be cases when the customer needs to contact a different line of business, or that your applicable policy will not let you to do exactly what they want.

Support policies should not prevent you to act in critical issues, and you should follow good will credit policies and empowerment levels according to your LOB standards.

Recognize serious circumstances that are outside your control and talk to a manager or advocate for what is right for your customer.

Important: Be reasonable and keep the conversation balanced. Do not choose the easy way because you do not feel comfortable with delivering a hard message.

How to analyze a scenario for which the customer’s issue cannot be resolved:

  1. Is it included in an existing process (for example, Make it Right, support, and solve scenarios)? Is guidance available in your LOB processes and procedures? If yes – follow ‘the letter’ of the procedure (for example, criteria for refund, MIR rules, and Office solve scenarios).
  2. Am I able to match this scenario to another scenario already included in a process?

    Prerequisite, understanding and assessing ‘the spirit’ of the procedure (vs the letter of the procedure) within – scenarios that could be considered as part of certain process or procedure although not necessarily defined as such. Procedures are not usually intended to cover 100% of scenarios that come in real life, but they should give a hint base on when that procedure would be applicable in terms of principles and criteria guidance for generally applicable situations. Advocates, team managers and SMEs are encouraged to read between the lines and find ways of matching their scenario with the spirit main purpose of the procedure in order to do what is right for the customer.
  3. Do I have a valid point to support an exception for this customer? Deciding when a scenario is 100% outside of any existing process, but based on the context gathered in the conversation and our being accountable for the customer’s expected behavior, you are empowered to ask for what is right for the customer.

Talk to your manager or escalate as appropriate relay the situation to justify your request.

Use SpeakUp! to share your feedback to help smooth the process for the next customers.

Exceptional out of process request that should be discussed with your management/site escalation team:

  • Microsoft error, including Microsoft Store support advocate error in handling of a customer scenario.
  • Sensitive or risk scenarios where there might be additional consequences for Microsoft or the customer. Examples of consequences for Microsoft (but not limited to): legal, financial, reputational etc.

Important: Build and articulate the story in your case notes, be clear and detailed in terms of why and how you are planning to solve the customer’s issue.

5.12 Seamless transfer or escalation

Before suggesting a transfer or making a referral, make sure that you identified the customer’s needs, has ownership of the case and focused on offering a solution on the spot.

While it is not always possible to avoid transfer, referral or escalation, make sure you take time to understand the issue first. Attempt basic troubleshoot using general resources and make a transfer to the last resort solution. You need to clearly explain why transfer or referral is needed while owning the issue, focusing on what you can do.

Examples of basic troubleshooting: Explain how to download, install and activate, where to find the link, what steps to follow, and how to use and send online support articles.

  • The purpose of guiding through online resources is to empower the customer to self-serve, accelerate learning and prevent customer dissatisfaction due to immediate transfer to technical support. Technical support numbers should be positioned as an alternative after the customer followed all steps available in the online resources.

6. Advise Me – As part of the expected behaviors that go across all phases of the CMF

Advise Me is an opportunity to share great ideas and experiences with your customer by providing relevant information, and creates additional value by leveraging the customer’s cues and addressing more than their immediate issue. A good sales advocate will use every opportunity to educate the customer and be helpful.

Checkpoint

Checkpoint details

Messaging specifics

Tips and techniques

6.1 Offer complete solution (attach, upsell)

Attach the product to the current or previous sale.

Upselling works 20 times better than cross-selling. Once customers have a product in mind, they often don't want to be distracted by something else, unless it enhances their initial choice.

 

Limit to only a few well targeted recommendations that provide a clear benefit to the customer.

Rule of 25

Never try to upsell or cross-sell items that are more than 25% of the original order. This may feel to customers like a second primary purchase, instead of an add-on, and it may deter the customer from making the purchase.

  • This should take place at the end of the sales cycle, when customers have already committed to the purchase or earlier as part of the proposed solution.

“The typical repair cost is… and most customers keep their devices for three years. It makes sense to take out warranty.”

6.2 Educate

Teach something new about a product already owned by the customer.

Use every opportunity to teach and generally be helpful. A good place to start is by always having a list of tips and tricks ready and give advice whenever possible.

  • The idea is to understand what your customer’s needs are and help them get the right solution. This can be more than just products or services; it can be advice or an insight.

Empower the customer through knowledge, providing the information they need to solve their own problems or to make an informed decision.

 

Use Advise Me campaigns resources to educate the customer. KB 448792 Advise Me campaign content.

6.3 Save

Ask why

Identify and address the reason behind the request.

  • Attempt to save on the current solution or migrate to another solution matching the customer’s needs.

 

“Sure, I can help you with the cancellation/return, but before proceeding may I know the reason you do not want the subscription or product anymore?”

Office save scenarios:

Forgot to cancel subscription: “Will you still need Office or at least have alternatives? The reason I asked is the fact that canceling your subscription will disable access to create and edit files on devices you have the software installed on and you can no longer access the files you have on OneDrive. Are you prepared for these limitations? We also have a monthly billing option if you prefer monthly charges instead.“

Free apps are available: “Although online applications are available, to set the right expectations, there are some limitations to using these versions. New versions upgrades are also included in the package you pay for yearly/monthly. Do you still wish to switch to a lifetime version by purchasing the office perpetual license?”

Does not use all features: “What Office applications did you need most? There are standalone options available for use too. Although unlike Office 365, installation limit and updates have their differences.”

7. Deliver solution/verify solution

This step is intended to confirm if the solution was based on the customer’s needs and expectations. A satisfied customer perceives the proposed resolution as adding value to their needs and creates excitement.

7.1 Set expectations for the next steps

Prevent future issues by addressing future concerns or risks beyond the immediate issue (next issue avoidance).

  • Explain what the customer should expect next to get the product and start using it.

Customers need information, from the moment they start interacting with you to days and months after making a purchase. Be aware, and share educational blog content, instructional customer facing articles, and regular communication with customers so they have the information they need to successfully use a product or service.

Device purchase

Setup your device.

Escalated case

Setup expectations on next steps and timelines, schedule a follow-up to keep the customer informed and give a final resolution.

7.2 Enforce product/solution usage

Being a trusted advisor for your customer includes making recommendations on further actions so the customer can successfully set up and use the product purchased. You should prepare the customer for the steps needed and share any available resources that would help decrease setup time and increase usage.

 

Use my device online resources should be recommended to customers in the following scenarios:

  • After making a purchase (POBO or customer guided purchase).
  • Customers contacting sales support after a purchase with questions on how to setup their device.

The purpose of guiding customers through online resources is to empower them to self-serve, accelerate learning and to prevent dissatisfaction due to immediate transfer to technical support. Technical support numbers are an alternative after the customer followed all steps available in the online resources.

7.3 Summarize solution and get customer consent

Summarization is a good step to ensure that your customers get the information needed, provide their consent on a purchase/transaction before being processed in the system, and understand what will happen next.

Customer’s consent on POBO and terms of sale

Making a purchase on behalf of the customer has immediate and/or recurrent impact on the customer’s financials, therefore customers need to be clearly informed about the products, quantity, total amount to be charged, and any automatic renewal options. Customers need to explicitly give consent and agree with Microsoft Terms of Sales and, if applicable, with Automatic renewal subscription terms.

Order summaries

For clarity purposes, complicated purchase ordering should generally be followed up with a summary to ensure that all information was captured correctly. For example, products in the order, configuration, price, taxes, shipping address, delivery times and next steps.

Complicated interaction

When calls are complicated or long, it becomes important to provide summary statements at the end so that the customer can make sure the information is accurate.

  

Repeating the information back to the customer (when it is correct), demonstrates that you are listening effectively and responding in real time to the customer’s situation. This builds trust and confidence.

Important: See KB 4578369 Procedure: Purchase on behalf of (POBO) Consent and KB 4053527 TUG: MST: Purchase on behalf of (POBO) for information on POBO and mandatory consent actions.

7.4 Confirmed fan

With every customer interaction we want to create Microsoft products fans. Pay attention to the customer’s verbal and non-verbal expressions, confirming their excitement and love of Microsoft products.

 

This checkpoint is mostly about active listening, and not talking.

7.5 Follow-up and nurture customer’s relationship

Sales advocates should ideally stay in touch with customers post sale, to make sure they are happy, that the product or service is functioning to their satisfaction and that there aren’t any issues.

By following up post-sale or keeping the customer updated with a transaction or escalation that takes time to be processed, you demonstrate commitment to the customer and create loyalty, which helps to generate repeated sales.

 

Good customer service steps include:

  • Providing updates on delivery.
  • Following up to ensure the goods have been received.
  • Checking on refund status.
  • Keeping customers informed with the progress of an escalation.
  • Saying thank you after concluding a transaction, makes a difference with the customer, and also adds a personal touch.

9. General conversation behaviors

Behavior

Details

Messaging specifics

Tips and techniques

9.1 Active listening

Are you truly hearing what your customer is saying or are you selectively hearing the parts you want to hear that match your understanding?

Remove any bias or assumptions and truly listen. This means paying attention to not only what they’re saying, but how they’re saying and what they’re not saying.

Give the customer your undivided attention, acknowledging what is being said. Non-verbal communication also speaks loudly. Let the customer know you are listening by providing verbal nods. Don’t start to mentally prepare a response while the customer is speaking, as this distracts from listening.

Active listening is about paying attention to what the customer is saying with focus, understanding, responding and remembering what is being said.

It’s not about repeating back exactly what the customer said.
  • Stay focused
  • Take notes
  • Ask questions
  • Avoid stereotypes
  • Avoid assumptions based on previous experiences with other customers.
  • Recap key facts

9.2 Create connection – Build rapport and trust

Rapport building is an integral part of the communication process. Lack or absence of a rapport can affect the outcome of any conversation. Good rapport means creating a comfortable state where all parties can converse freely and comfortably.

Leading with empathy is the foundation to building genuine trust and connection with customers. Empathy in sales is the willingness and ability to put yourself into the mental and emotional position of a prospective customer during the sales process. Leading with empathy allows you to manage your emotions, and accurately recognize the emotions of others. It can mean the difference between a good salesperson and a great salesperson. When you connect with a customer on a higher emotional level, more likely they are to trust and believe in your ability to help.

  • Trust is the single most important factor for customers when making purchases.

There are four factors to build trust, improving credibility (can we believe what you say), reliability (can we depend on your actions), intimacy (do we feel safe sharing information with you) and lowering self-orientation (in your interaction and motives are you focused on yourself or on the other person).

Intimacy and self-orientation are the two of the biggest levers of trustworthiness, and the areas of most opportunity.

  • The use of language and vocabulary impacts customer experience.
  • Top sales performers use more inclusive and collaborative language.
  • Top performers use words such as “definitely”, “absolutely” or “certainly” (for example, “our product can definitely solve this” is better than “our product might be able to solve this”).

Less need to build a rapport in a messaging conversation.

Keep the discussion simple, natural, and at point, while adapting to your customer’s communication style.

Note: Less rapport does not mean being robotic.

To humanize chat conversations, you should look for cues and opportunities to inject something personal into the conversation. For example, if the customer asks for advice or recommendation, you can share your own experience and excitement about the product.

  • Inject humor if and where appropriate, ensuring it is natural and follows the flow of the conversation, the customer’s tone and mood.

While you should display your human side in messaging, it is recommended to avoid using fluffy verbiage and being overly apologetic to customers.

Be authentic.

Rapport building starts with your welcoming phrase

Mirror the customer’s communication style

Use words that the customer uses in their conversation, especially any adjectives. They have chosen to use these words, so they have a relevance to the content and an alternative word may not have the same meaning for them. Skilled advisors should also be able to match their customers pace and tone, demonstrating commonality and helping to build rapport.

Show empathy

Showing empathy creates a sense of trust and mutual understanding. For example: Language such as “I understand what you mean”, “I can see where you are coming from”, “That must have made you feel really good”, “I understand why you would think that way”.

Change your perspective - Get out of your own head and into your customer’s. Being able to say to them, “I understand the frustration you’re feeling” means more than saying, “I’m sorry you’re frustrated.”

When using empathy statements, it is good to use personal pronouns, active verbs and show authenticity.

“I can understand how frustrating it is when…”

“How can I make this process easier for you?”

“I imagine how upsetting it is to…”

“I can help with that.”

“If I skip anything important, please stop me and let me know.”

Pause before responding

If you find yourself in a situation where a lot is riding on your response, take a moment to pause. Change your perspective and see the situation from their point of view. Usually, this can diffuse your initial reaction and allow you to respond from a place of understanding.

Go off script

Reading from a script can be a barrier to building rapport. Customers often disengage when they can tell the conversation is scripted. If it’s necessary to read from a script, put your own personality into it. This will help sound like it’s coming from you.

Calibrate the rapport

New sales advocates are often overly sensitive to the time of a potential buyer, so they dive right in without an ice breaker and it doesn't go well. Others can spend too much time chatting, while the customer may just want to get down to business. You should read the customer and find the right amount of rapport focused conversation.

Read the culture

Adjust the approach depending on the customer. Be aware of cultural nuances and how best to respond.

Create trust and establish credibility

Develop deep knowledge of the products and services and potentially the industry and be passionate about the subject. But also, be willing to say “I don’t know” if this is the honest answer.

Boost reliability

State expectations upfront and report on them regularly. Follow through on promises and communicate if something goes wrong by taking responsibility.

Promote intimacy

Use the customer’s name, share something personal (makes you human and more interesting), tune into their tone, emotion, and mood.

9.3 Tone and pace

Engage at the speed and timing the customer wants by paying attention to their cues, matching customer’s tone, and pace, and responding to their sense of urgency.

Use a friendly, conversational tone throughout the entire interaction.

 

9.4 Hold and silent time

Use hold instead of keeping customers on mute. Let the customer now before putting them on hold, setting expectations on what you are searching for and how long it will take.

Use this time to search for information or to process a transaction in the tool, and keep customers informed on what you are actioning on their behalf to avoid silent times.

When to use hold instead of mute:

  • The customer hears music when on hold, hence he knows that he is kept on hold and someone will return to the call.
  • The customer hears complete silent when on mute, hence you should avoid using it as customers might think the call got disconnected.

Important: Mute is only used for clearing your throat, sneezing, or sipping water. For any other reasons, use hold.

Longer times between the lines are normal for a messaging conversation.

No need to fill in between the lines, if expectations on time through resolution are set.

Note: Hold time is not applicable to messaging.

When using hold:

  • Ask for permission, wait for customer agreement, and thank the customer.
  • Set waiting time expectations.
  • Explain the reason for the wait.
  • Refresh, then return to the customer regularly.

Nobody likes to wait. It’s better to be specific and direct by saying: “Please hold for a minute so that I can speak with my supervisor and get back to you with an answer”.

“I am now getting into the system to start processing your order/refund/data check.” “I am now getting to the last step and it will just take another second”. “I am almost done [explain what you are doing]”

  • When you come back from hold is not recommended to say Hello. Thank the customer for waiting and revert to discussion.

9.5 Create fans

Our goal is to create, inspire and build a community of Microsoft fans who are empowered to achieve more.

Usage + Love + Advocacy = FAN

 

Microsoft commitment to fan creation customers key expected behaviors:

  • Communicate effectively
  • Be accountable
  • Be resourceful
  • Be empathetic

To create fans, you need to show customers that you are a fan also. Romance products, love the products, become a product expert, and share your admiration with the customer to help them to also fall in love with Microsoft products.

9.6 Create emotion

Emotionally connected customers buy more, visit more often, exhibit less price sensitivity, pay more attention to your communications, follow your advice, and recommend you more.

 

Customers become more valuable at each step of the emotional connection pathway as they transition from: 1) being unconnected to 2) being highly satisfied to 3) perceiving brand differentiation to 4) being fully connected.

9.7 Set expectations

Setting realistic expectations, helps win the customer’s trust. If you can successfully manage these expectations, you will better meet and even exceed them. As a result, you get to have a better connection and longer relationship with that customer.

Always keep the customer aware of what you are doing and how long it will take to give them an answer. Have a transfer picked up, an escalation resolved, and make a transaction to have a product delivered. Customers love to know what to expect, and have those expectations met.

If something happens, it’s better to act on it, let the customer know what happened and proceed with fixing the issue by making it right.

In case you need to take additional steps until you provide a resolution, let the customer know. Remember to give a time estimate whenever possible.
  • Openly discuss solutions.
  • Give clear timelines.
  • Be transparent, honest, and avoid misinforming the customer.
  • Eliminate unpleasant surprises by making the customer aware of what they should expect.
  • Proactively educate the customer on capabilities and limitations. This is the best practice to ensure that the customers can easily find information on various products and services we offer.
  • Follow-up regularly.
  • When a service failure occurs, apologize and carry out the service recovery to make things right again.
