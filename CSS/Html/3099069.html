1. Customer holds

1.1 Reasons to put a customer on hold

  • When redirecting or escalating the customer
  • When more research time is needed
  • When more time is needed for a troubleshooting step

Return to top

1.2 Guidelines for advocates

Office and Windows

  • Explain to the customer why you are placing them on hold and that you will return promptly.
    • The hold time should not exceed three minutes.
    • If more time is needed, check in with the customer after three minutes and ask them for more time.
  • You may put the customer on hold multiple times, but the cumulative total hold time should not exceed five minutes.
    • If you need more time, consult with your TM or SME to consider escalation.
  • If the customer is unresponsive or disconnected during the hold time, follow the guidance in section 2.

Accounts & Billing

  • Explain to customer why you are placing them on hold and that you will return promptly.
    • The hold time should not exceed two minutes.
    • If more time is needed, check in with customer after one minute and ask them for more time.
  • If the customer is unresponsive or disconnected during the hold time, follow the guidance in section 2.

Return to top

1.3 Customer requests to put you on hold

  • Ask the customer how long they expect the wait to be and set the expectation with the customer that you can wait up to three minutes.
  • If the customer does not return within the specified time, hold for an additional three minutes before hanging up.
  • Close the SR as Closed: Unresolved.

2. Disconnected call

  1. If the call gets disconnected, callback the customer immediately.
  2. If you are unable to reach the customer, complete the Incident form and send the customer a closing email when the customer email is captured:
    Subject: Disconnected Call
  3. Close the SR as Closed: Unresolved.
  4. Do not callback if they disconnected after the offer of paid service.


Chat: Follow the procedure in KB 3018106 Procedure: Chat Handling

3. Callbacks

3.1 Guidelines for Office and Windows advocates

Callbacks permitted scenarios:

Outbound calls are allowed for assistance with no-charge issues and paid services (one-time paid incidents and subscriptions). Callbacks are useful for various situations. A callback made by Tier 1 or Tier 2 is appropriate in most instances. Below are some examples:

  • When you have exhausted all knowledge and resources and have been unable to identify a resolution
  • When you and the customer have been disconnected unexpectedly
  • When specific direction is published on the Consumer Support Agent Blog for an emerging issue that requires the collection of diagnostic information
  • When a customer makes a request for a callback
    • If the customer is rushed and cannot continue to stay on the call, offer to call the customer back.
    • If the customer declines a callback and wants to call Microsoft Support back at their own convenience, then close the SR as Closed: Unresolved and reopen the case when the customer calls back.

It's also appropriate to schedule a callback on behalf of your manager when one is not available. This would be ideal for a customer that is unhappy, irate, or disgruntled and asks to speak to a supervisor.

If the customer says they are not ready to troubleshoot and will call us back when they are ready, then you can set up a call back with the customer. If they decline the callback, close the SR as Closed: Unresolved and reopen the case when the customer calls back.

If you are calling a customer back in Japan, see KB 892833 Procedure: Call recording.

Return to top

Callbacks not permitted scenarios:

There are a few instances when a callback is not permitted. Do not schedule or perform a callback in the following circumstances:

  • Callback to witness or confirm resolution
  • Callback to re-witness resolution (for example, We fixed your issue the last time, but we want to call back to make sure the issue is still fixed)
  • Callback to ensure the customer is happy or satisfied
  • Callback to allow agents to work in a remote session (LMI) while the customer is offline (for example, advocate ends the phone call or chat but continues to work on the LMI session. The advocate then calls the customer back once they completed their work in a remote session)
  • Callback a Paid Offer Declined scenario, where the customer has been provided self-help options, such as a KB article or redirection to the Communities
  • Callback to complete a sale due to credit card failure or credit card not with customer condition: Advise the customer to call us back at a time convenient to them

3.3 Tier 2 Callback procedure

It’s important that you complete these steps as soon as you reach the customer. If delayed, it will be reported as a late follow-up. Contact the customer at the time that was promised.

If you can reach the customer and resolve their issue

Use Case Scheduler indicating the issue is resolved. On the Case Scheduler, do the following to close the Call Back:

  1. In the section for Result of Callback leave this at the default of either Non-Response or Missed – Reschedule. Due to system functionality this cannot be reset and is not used to indicate if the call back was missed or made on time.
  2. Select Issue Resolved
  3. Select Confirm
  4. Select Close
  5. Select Confirm and then Save on the Service Request (SR)

If a callback was scheduled and the customer cannot be reached
Scenario 1: Customer has a voicemail that you can leave a message:

  1. Leave a voice message and tell the customer you will attempt to call them again the next day. When leaving a voice message, state the following:
    • Your name
    • The reason why you are calling
    • When they can expect you to call again
    • The Microsoft phone number where they can reach us
    • That you will be calling back the next day along with the date and timeframe you will be calling
  2. Ensure that the Result of the callback field is set to Non-Response
  3. Select Issue Resolved in the case scheduler.
  4. Schedule a follow up for the next day in the Call Back Scheduler.

Scenario 2: Customer has no voicemail; your call is unanswered:

  • Send Strike email to the customer and re-schedule the call for the following day.
  • Keep the Service Request open until the re-scheduled call.
  • Select Issue Resolved in the case scheduler.
  • Schedule a follow up for the next day in the Call Back Scheduler.

Scenario 3: You have attempted several times to call back, but customer can’t be reached or does not call back:

  1. Send the Strike email to the customer, letting them know you were unable to reach them and to contact you back at their earliest convenience.
  2. In Result of Callback leave as default of either Non-Response or Missed – Reschedule.
  3. Select Issue Resolved
  4. Select Confirm
  5. Select Close
  6. Select Confirm and then Save the Service Request
  7. On the Incident form change Status to Closed: Unresolved and select Complete.
  8. Add notes on the case as Lost Contact

Notes:

  • Use the standard strike email template in ASD
  • There should be three callbacks (every 30 mins) during the schedule callback time frame
  • The case should be closed as Lost Contact on the 3rd day if still no answer

If you are not able to make a callback

If you are going to be out of the office or unable to make the callback, you must advise your supervisor/lead that you will not be able to make the callback. This will ensure that someone on the team can reach out to the customer on your behalf.

Customer phone number is not available

  1. Send the customer an email requesting a phone number we can use to schedule a call back to work on the issue.
  2. When the customer provides their phone number, make sure to document their phone number in the SR.
  3. Select Issue Resolved in the case scheduler and schedule a new follow up entry via the case scheduler for the next business day with the same time interval. Mark the follow up type as e-mail.
  4. If the customer doesn't respond to the email within a day, send a closing email to let them know you were unable to reach them and to contact us back at their earliest convenience. Make sure to select issue resolved to clear all pending follow ups then close the case.

Customer no longer needs a callback

There are times when a customer will need to cancel a callback because they resolved the issue themselves, or with the help of a third-party (for example, a friend). In such cases, you will need to resolve all pending follow-ups.

If there are pending follow ups, make sure to select issue resolved to clear all pending follow ups. Afterwards, close the case.

4. Escalated cases (Office and Windows advocates only)

4.1 New callback time

If the customer is contacting Microsoft via an escalated Service Request (SR) to re-schedule the callback, take ownership of the SR as normal, re-establish the callback time, then re-escalate the case.

Office/Windows – Refer to Section 2.3 in KB 3046180 Tool User Guide: Assisted Support Desktop (ASD)

Return to top

4.2 Missed callback

If the customer is contacting Microsoft via an escalated SR because they didn’t receive a callback as promised, take ownership of the SR, document the missed callback, and live-escalate. A missed callback is considered an auto-escalation scenario.

For more information, see KB 2967480 Procedure: Windows: Technical support escalations.

Return to top

4.3 Confirming callback

If the customer is contacting Microsoft to confirm their callback appointment. Do not take ownership of the SR. Select Edit instead and add a comment using “Edit Comments Only” mode.

Refer to KB 3121083 Procedure: Transfer of Case Ownership (TOCO Request).

5. Ghost/spam/wrong number

Ghost/spam/wrong number contacts - There is no caller on the line, no responsive callers on incoming calls, spam recordings, or the customer has not responded to the initial greeting or provided sufficient information to appropriately code.

  1. If you fail to hear a response to your greeting, try getting the customer’s attention again two more times.
    • Wait approximately 10 seconds between each attempt.
    • Do not repeat the opening greeting three times total, be creative: “Hello? This is < your name > can you hear me?” “Hi, are you still there?”.
  2. If you fail to hear a response after a total of three greetings, use the following sample script and then disconnect the call:

    "I’m very sorry, but I am unable to hear you. If you can hear me, please contact us again or go to support.microsoft.com. Thank you for contacting Microsoft Support.”
  3. See the following guidance for coding a case as Ghost:
  • A&B: Select Ghost Contact in [Ghost / Spam] field
  • Office and Windows: KB 2967100 Procedure: Windows and Office: ASD Incident Form coding
  1. Test calls - There is a caller from Microsoft performing internal testing or other organizations that choose to do their own test calls. These can be treated as a ghost contact.
