1. Overview

Keywords are words or phrases that trigger ads to display. If a user's search query or other input matches a keyword that is associated with an ad, the ad can then display with the search results. For example, keywords could be any of the following:

  • Shoes
  • Boat cruise vacation
  • Tennis lessons in New York

After creating keywords, advertisers then need to bid on the keywords for their ads to display. Microsoft Advertising offers three match type options: exact, phrase, and broad match. For more information see What are keyword match types, and how do I use them?. These options give advertisers greater control over how their ads get matched to their potential customers’ search queries. Advertiser can have independent bids on each match type for a keyword.

Advertisers are bidding against each other to get their ads into the space they want. If the advertiser bids on the keyword "shoe," they will compete against the bids and quality of the other advertisers who are also bidding on "shoe."

3. ﻿Keyword match types

Keyword match types help Microsoft Advertising determine how closely a search query or other input must match the keyword. Generally, the more precise advertisers require the match to be, the higher the conversion rates tend to be, while impressions tend to decrease. Finding the right balance between conversions and impressions can help maximize the return on investment of a campaign.

Use these resources to learn about match types and how they work:

  • What are keyword match types, and how do I use them?
  • Add the words people are searching for as keywords
  • Using the broad match modifier

In addition to match types, Bing also uses a process known as entity detection to pick up multiple-word phrases that should be treated as a unique term (for example, the keyword “Bill Gates” would be considered one unique term, and ideally should not trigger an add that uses either “Bill” or “Gates” as keywords).

This is a back-end logic function that you may hear referenced during escalations to support teams; however, it is not something we should discuss with customers or guarantee, as entity detection is subjective and not something we actively enforce.

3.1﻿ Close variants

So that advertisers don’t miss out on potential customers, we show ads for close variations of their keywords. This applies to all keyword match types, including exact match. 

Close variants mean that an ad will also show when the query is a close grammatical variation of a keyword. The close variants feature is designed not to change the intent shown by the user.

This is a common practice across Microsoft Advertising and Google Ads. For more information, and supported markets, see the ‘Close keyword variations’ section of What are keyword match types, and how do I use them?. You can also share the following OneSheet with customers if they wish to know more about this functionality.

The only exception is US pharmaceutical keywords, which will not match exact/phrase close variants to avoid contradicting federal compliance guidelines.

See the table for a complete list of close variant types and examples:

Variant type

Example

Plural vs. singular
  • “All inclusive resort” vs. “all inclusive resorts”
  • “Perfume” vs. “perfumes”
  • “Data recovery program” vs. “data recovery programs”

Stemming
  • “Send condolences” vs. “sending condolences”
  • “Discount clothing” vs. “discounted clothing”
  • “Grow roses” vs. “growing roses”

Misspellings & common spelling variations
  • “Car insurance” vs. “car isnurance”
  • “Halloween costumes” vs. “Halloween coustumes”
  • “Violet color paint” vs. “Violet colour paint”

Spacing - Word blending/splitting
  • “Xbox” vs. “x box”; “websites” vs. “web sites”

Abbreviations/Acronyms
  • Redmond, Washington vs. Redmond, WA

Certain punctuation
  • “Stop-smoking help” vs. “Stop smoking help”
  • “Italian cafés London” vs. “Italian cafes London”

  • “teriyaki chicken” vs “chicken teriyaki”
  • “gluten intolerance symptoms” vs “symptoms gluten intolerance”

  • “Hotel in new york” vs. “Hotel new york"

Synonyms
  • “normal range for blood sugar” vs. “glucose levels normal range”

Implied words or same search intent
  • “Contoso airlines” vs. “flights Contoso airlines”

Close variation messaging:

Advertisers may ask why their keywords are being matched against variations, especially for exact phrase and BMM terms. In these instances, use the following messaging:

"Close variant matching uses machine-learning to associate keywords with search query opportunities, in cases where the search query is deemed to be a close grammatical match, or is a strong match with the search user’s query intent. This ensures you are reaching your target audience without the need to use many variations on keywords such as plurals or acronyms.
If you’re concerned about matching against certain terms, you can use negative keywords to avoid being associated. We’d also recommend using the ‘search query report’ in Microsoft Advertising to look at what keywords are trigging your ad, so that these can be added as negative terms."

3.2 ﻿Broad Match Modifier (BMM)

Sometimes advertisers will want to restrict how liberally Microsoft Advertising matches those closely related terms. For example, let's say you create a broad match keyword “Hawaii Hotels”. A query for “Hawaii Rentals” might also trigger your ads, given that "rentals" is closely related to “hotels”. But you own a hotel and don't want traffic from searchers looking for rental properties. The solution is to add the "+" broad match modifier to your keyword to make it “Hawaii +Hotels”. This tells Microsoft Advertising that the word “hotels” (or one of its close variations) should be in the query for your ads to be eligible to be served. For advertisers who are sensitive to broad match (especially on brand terms), BMM can be used.

Note: In certain uncommon cases, a word that has the + modifier may have no equivalent present in a matching query, or an alternate word very similar in meaning will be present instead. The + modifier is not an absolute guaranty that the query will contain the word, but it makes it much more likely that a matching query will contain it.

BMM best practices:

  • Broad Match Modifier should be the last resort in terms of broad match controls.
  • Leverage broad match modifier to set restrictive limits, keeping in mind that it can block up to 90% of broad match potential volume.
  • For more selective limits that block less volume, use negative keywords.
  • For clients who are sensitive to their brand terms, it is recommended that they use BMM on their brand term to increase likelihood it appears in the query. This will ensure their brand keywords will not show for queries that do not contain that brand.
  • Special characters are removed from keywords using normalization. If you want a phone number or product to appear in a query, place a "+" in front of each term. Example: +12 +34 +56
  • Make sure to include proper spacing when applying BMM to your terms. Do not include a space before the "+" and make sure to include a space between words. Example: “+new +shoes” vs “+ new+shoes”.
  • Putting a “+” on every token in your keyword will heavily limit traffic especially for keywords with more than three tokens. Optimize the number of BMM tokens as needed.
  • There is no need to duplicate tokens or order for BMM. Example: If you bid on “+nike +shoes”, there is no need to bid on “+shoes +nike” or “+shoes +shoes +nike”, as they will already be covered.

Broad match best practices to take your campaigns to new heights:

Step 1

 

Ensure your campaigns are opted into broad match to get maximum reach without sacrificing quality with other match types.
Step 2

 

Dial broad match up or down depending on conversion value by bidding explicitly for each match type relative to performance.
Step 3

 

To further enhance control, use negative keywords to block unwanted query matches. Refine with additional negative keywords as needed.
Step 4

 

Remember to leverage the Opportunities tab and Intelligence tool for more customized broad match keyword suggestions.
Step 5

 

As a last resort, leverage broad match modifier to set restrictive limits, keeping in mind that broad match modifiers can block up to 90% of broad match potential volume.

Resource: Best Practices to drive volume using broad match

Broad match includes smart match, which automatically includes words that are closely related to the keywords.

  • For example, a search query for “red carnation” might also display ads bidding on the keyword “red flower” because Microsoft Advertising automatically identifies carnation as a type of flower.

3.3 ﻿Bad match investigations

Sometimes the keyword match made by our algorithms will be considered a bad match by an advertiser. This could be due to unexpected spikes in traffic, irrelevant search term matches, or inappropriate matches.

We can escalate to our partners for a review of these bad matches, to determine if these are a genuine bad match or if this is by-design behavior, using the bad match review process:

Bad match review process

  1.  Obtain specific details about campaign, ad group, keywords, and timeframe.
  2.  Download the search term report for max. previous 60 days (from the point at which the customer has contacted CSS).

Note:  For DSA campaigns, remember to run the Dynamic Search Ad Search Term Report instead.

  1. Make sure to include proper SQR report format (SQR with KWID and date range included). This will allow support teams to reduce escalation turnaround time and give them the ability to reproduce the issue. Ensure the following columns are ALL included in the report you provide in your escalation, and do not remove any default columns:
  • Account (DSA Investigations only)
  • Account ID
  • Account Number
  • Ad Group
  • Avg. CPC (DSA Investigations only)
  • Avg. Position (DSA Investigations only) 
  • Bid Match Type
  • Campaign
  • Clicks
  • Conversions
  • Conversion Rate (DSA Investigations only)
  • CPA (DSA Investigations only)
  • CTR (DSA Investigations only)
  • Customer Name
  • Date
  • Delivered Match Type
  • Dynamic Ad Target (DSA investigations only)
  • Final URL (DSA Investigations only)
  • Headline (DSA Investigations only)
  • Impressions
  • Keyword
  • Keyword ID
  • Language (DSA Investigations only)
  • Revenue
  • Search Term
  • Spend

Note: Ensure the Aggregation option is set to Day.

  1. Highlight the Top 500 (based on spend) search queries that are considered to be bad matches, or ask the requestor to provide this information.
    Note: The support team only reviews highlighted matches. If this cannot be done, specifically call out the nature of the customer’s complaint about the bad match key words. Example: The customer is upset that the keyword airplane is matching to search terms with only the word plane.
  2. Create an escalation with the market-specific support team to help block these irrelevant matches. The escalation path varies by region. Make sure to choose the correct market when escalating in ASD: The system will automatically route to the correct escalation team provided the market is correct. 
    • EMEA: Ad Quality team – nqs@microsoft.com
    • Rest of world: Global Support - Adsupport@microsoft.com

Use the following ASD coding for clarity:

  • EMEA:

T2 Receiving Team = Network Quality Services (NQS), T2 Sub-Team = Matching

  • Rest of world:

T2 Receiving Team = Ad Platform & Tools, T2 Sub-Team = Campaign Set Up Technical Support

  • SLA Note: There is no ‘fixed’ SLA for matching requests, though most escalations should be resolved within 9-13 calendar days.

Important: 

Do not use the term “Invalid Clicks”, either in your subject line or body of your escalation, when requesting a bad match review! Global Support have automated rules that will filter invalid queries to a different team, which can cause delays and confusion in ticket handling.

  1. In your escalation, note the timeframe and ensure you attach the search term report (including all columns outlined above) that you generated earlier.
  2. The support team will review the report and will resolve the ticket with the appropriate message and suggestions. The escalation owner will share the list of keywords that engineering reviewed, along with a suggested goodwill/credit amount based on their review. ​​​

Note:

  1. Due to privacy and data integrity, the team cannot specifically highlight which terms were labelled as bad matches. Use the following messaging if you are pressured by the advertiser to share this logic:

“Engineering does not share the breakdown of judgement used in match-breaking and refund calculations. This policy is due to the closely guarded nature of our proprietary algorithms and guidelines used in the matching process.”

  1. The goodwill/credit amount suggested by the team is based only on the keywords reviewed. This value is based on whether they deemed the matching to be bad match or by design and should be considered a suggestion only – CSS is ultimately responsible for determining and issuing credit amounts.

Internal note: Following a bad match escalation, human labellers judge these escalated matches on a scale from Bad to Excellent. Engineering will ban the bad ads and patterns are fed into model improvements.

We recommend advertisers use scheduled reports for brand/top accounts and monitor performance on a weekly/monthly basis, to quickly address any irrelevant matches by adding negative terms on the appropriate match type.  

Achieve More: When presenting the results to a customer, remember to review the report and determine negatives and BMMs to optimize and maintain control over ad delivery for matching keywords.

Large scale requests: Depending on the size of the bad match escalation, Tier 2 (T2) may not always be able to review all the highlighted terms that you escalate. In this scenario, they will provide you with:

  • The credit amount they recommend.
  • The number of keywords that were reviewed.

CSS can then calculate the amount to be refunded to the customer using the following logic:

  1. Add up the total spend of all reviewed keywords to identify the Total Spend (Reviewed).
  2. Divide the Credit Amount by the Total Spend (Reviewed), and multiply by 100, to determine the % Refund (Reviewed).
  3. Add the total spend of all suspected bad-match keywords in your initial escalation, to determine the Total Spend (Escalated).
  4. Divide Total Spend (Escalated) by 100, then multiply by % Refund (Reviewed), to calculate % Final Refund.

This represents a proportional refund based on the number of keywords Tier 2 confirmed as bad-matches vs. the number of keywords they were able to review. 

Appeals/Disputes – If a customer disagrees with the decision made by the review team, they must provide valid justification as to why they feel it’s a bad match (for example, “I have this term set as a negative keyword already but am still getting traffic” or “I don’t sell this product”). Highlight the term(s) in dispute and provide via the original ticket to re-open it. Note: Match breaking is applied to the entire marketplace, not just the customer’s own keyword, so there may be cases where we need to leverage negative terms (as described earlier) rather than rely on match breaking.

3.4 Refunds for bad matches and additional goodwill scenario

Confirmed bad matches can be refunded at 100% of the amount provided to you by the escalation support teams. This should be coded in the Adjustments Tab under the drop-down System Issue.

In addition, if an advertiser has concerns about irrelevant traffic that was deemed to be by design and it is the first time they have reached out, review the customer’s account for negative keyword opportunities, add these to their account, and provide a goodwill credit for the top 500 performing keywords only, using the guidance outlined in KB 3173012 Process: Microsoft Advertising: Credits, Refunds, & Rebills. This can be treated as user education as the customer was unfamiliar with how to use negative terms to prevent this behavior from occurring.

This should be coded in the Adjustments tab under the drop-down Goodwill.

Remember to educate the advertiser on how to use search term reports to identify future negative keyword opportunities. 

Note: Make sure the customer adds negative terms and applies any other suggested optimization (for example, increasing match type specificity) before issuing a refund.

6. Spelling correction

When a search is performed on Bing, the query may be corrected if there is a very high likelihood that the query is misspelled. This occurs before any keywords are matched to the query. Because of this, certain bids on misspelled keywords may get less traffic than expected since the misspelled queries they would match to will get spell corrected. This does not mean that it is not worth bidding on misspelled keyword variants; in some cases, the query will not be corrected, and Bing will try matching using the “close variant” matching logic described above.

Special Note: Negative keywords are matched to the raw query (the actual un-normalized query entered by the user before any spell correction is applied).

Whether or not the query is spell corrected, it will then be normalized.

7. Keyword normalization 

All Microsoft Advertising keywords and queries are normalized before being matched together; that is, any symbols are generally removed from both keywords and queries before determining whether a keyword is a match to a query, and letter case is ignored. Accented characters are preserved.

Although negative keywords are also normalized, they are compared to the un-normalized search query. So, a negative keyword like Bing would not block the query “@Bing”.

8. ﻿Negative keywords  

Reduce your advertising costs and keep your ad from appearing in searches that are not related to your business with negative keywords. Specify search terms or phrases that you do not want matching to your ads. For example, if you bid on "car gear" but don’t want to target potential customers who want car insurance, use "insurance" as a negative keyword.

Return to top

8.1 How negative keywords work

  • Negative keywords can be set at the campaign level or the ad group level. ​Negatives can also be added to shared lists which can be associated with campaigns.
  • When evaluating whether a keyword matches a query, Bing checks to see if a negative keyword exists in the Microsoft Advertising UI, in the following order:
    1. Shared Negative Keyword List
    2. Campaign Negative Keyword List
    3. Ad Group Negative Keyword List

A negative in any of these places that matches the query will prevent the keyword from serving an ad.

  • Each negative keyword can contain up to 100 characters.
  • Each negative keyword list can have up to 5,000 negative keywords.

Phrase matching is the default match type for negative keywords at Microsoft Advertising. Negatives without a specific matching assignment will be applied as phrase match. ​Negative phrase matches will block both the exact query and any query containing the negative keyword.

Negative keywords are normalized before comparing them to the un-normalized query. See section 7, Keyword normalization.

Other than normalization, negative keywords are not otherwise modified. Only the precise negative keyword will be filtered out. Variants (such as plurals), synonyms, and common misspellings of the negative keyword are not filtered out. To have variants, synonyms, and common misspellings filtered out, you will need to enter each one as a separate negative keyword.

See Learn about using negative keywords to get to the right customer for more information.

8.2 Negative keywords best practices

Be sure to use shared negative lists for terms you will never want to serve any ads for, regardless of the keyword.

  • Many advertisers start by creating a global list and then associate that list to all their campaigns in the account. 

  • Take care that the items in your global list don’t conflict with keywords you have in your campaigns. 

  • Shared lists save time by allowing you to enter items once and then apply them to many other campaigns with just a few clicks.

To further fine tune, add negatives to specific campaign and ad group lists based on the results you see in the search terms reports for those campaigns.

  • Pay close attention to how many impressions you are getting for matches you may not want. It may be excessive to add negatives to prevent matches for which you are getting only a couple of impressions a month, and no clicks.
  • Know when to use negative exact match vs. negative phrase match. Negative phrase keywords are very powerful, since they prevent your ad from delivering for any query that contains the phrase, and it is easy to inadvertently block traffic you might want.
  • Remember that impressions blocked by negative keywords do not count as part of your voice in the share of voice report. If you’ve recently added some negatives and your impressions are reduced more than expected, use the change history report to identify the negative keywords you have added, and use the search terms report to identify queries for which you were previously getting a lot of traffic but are not now.
  • If you are interested in segmenting your traffic by delivered match type into different ad groups and campaigns, Microsoft Advertising will typically prefer bidded exact match keywords over other types from an account when they are a match to the query. Note: Microsoft Advertising does not currently have a strict preference for bidded phrase match over bidded broad, when the keywords are in different ad groups and both keywords are a match to the query. This feature is currently only available in US & FR markets. 

Run the negative keyword conflict report occasionally to identify cases in which you may have negatives that are blocking core traffic that might be useful to you.
