1.2 Why did this happen?

When we see someone sign in to a customer’s Microsoft account from an unfamiliar location, it can be a symptom of someone trying to break into their account. To help keep their account safe, we put a block on the account that requires an additional security check for any sign-in from that location.

Return to top

1.3 How do I fix this?

If the customer is travelling or attempting to sign in from a location they don’t normally sign in from, they will be asked to use a security code to confirm that the person signing in from that location is really the account owner. See Section 3.1.

If the customer received this message but did not sign in from a new location or device, this may be a sign of an attempt to break into their account and the customer should take steps to rule that out or protect their account. See Section 3.2.

2. Unusual sign-in activity customer experience

1. On attempted sign-in, the customer receives a notification that requests the use of security information to verify account ownership. [Asset 4513206]
2. The customer may select to receive the code via an email message, phone call, text message, or even via an authenticator app that has been designated as security info for their Microsoft account.

After selecting a code delivery method, the customer will also be prompted to add info to verify the address or phone number that code is being sent to. To do so, they will type either the first part of the email address, as hinted at by masked info, or the final four digits of the phone number, as hinted at by the masked info.

Once the security info is filled in, select Send code.		 [Asset 4513207]
3. The customer should receive a security code via phone or email and can then enter that code into the following page that pops up. [Asset 4513208]
4. The customer will then be able to access services and sign in from that location without any other interrupts.

The customer may also receive an email notification alerting them to the unusual sign-in activity, as shown here.		 [Asset 4513217]

3. Things To Try

3.1 If the customer signed in from a different location than they usually do

Ask if the customer has signed in using a device they haven’t used before or if they have signed in from a new location, such as a friend’s house, a library, or maybe their workplace.

If the customer has signed in from someplace new, they will need to clear the unusual activity state. To do so, the customer will be prompted to use a code sent to one of their pieces of security info. If the customer is having difficulty because they don't have access to their security info, you can do the following:

  1. Ask if the customer has access to a tablet or laptop that they normally sign-in with. If they do, try signing in with that device from that location. If that device was set to be a "trusted device" they should bypass the interrupt. Read more about Trusted Devices.
  2. If customer left their alternative contact phone at home, see if a family member or friend with access to the phone can relay the security code sent to that phone.
  3. If there is no way to access security info, the customer will regain access once they return to their usual location and sign in or, if absolutely necessary, they can use the “Recover your Microsoft account” form to get back in.  (See KB 3123259 Procedure: Microsoft account: The "Recover your Microsoft account" form for recovery form instructions).

3.2 If the customer did not sign in from a different location

Have the customer select Review recent activity in the email that was sent. This will show a log of recent sign in locations.
  • Have the customer check to make sure they don’t know why a sign in from that location would occur
  • Ask if the customer got a new phone from a different provider or if any new apps were installed that might try to sign in as the customer
    a. Some apps that sign in on behalf of the customer include email apps and social media apps tied to a Microsoft account.
    b. Apps that sign in on behalf of the customer will need to ask for permissions to do so when they are installed and will prompt the customer for their Microsoft account and password, so ask if the customer remembers having to enter their password when they set up any Windows Phone or Windows apps.
  • If the customer does not know why a sign in would occur from a different location, strongly suggest that the customer reset their password and check for unusual activity on any other accounts that they might have.
 If the customer indicates that they suspect their account was compromised, advise them of the following:
If apps, services, or subscriptions were purchased with this account:
  1. Sign-in to account.microsoft.com
  2. Select Order history and make sure there were no new transactions that you didn't make or expect.
  3. Select Billing info and make sure your billing info wasn't changed.
If customer indicates that they see unauthorized charges, refer to KB 930018 Procedure: How to handle account takeover on a Microsoft account for instructions.
If the customer uses OneDrive to store and share files, photos, or other data:
  1. Sign-in to OneDrive.live.com
  2. Select Shared on the side menu
  3. Review the folders and files that are being shared to make sure files are not being shared with people you don't know or that any shares you made weren't deleted.
If the customer uses an Outlook or Hotmail email account:
  1. Sign-in to outlook.com
  2. Select the gear-shaped Settings icon, then select Options.
  3. Check the security settings and restore any that might have been lost or changed.
  4. Check the deleted mail folder and Contacts lists to make sure unusual messages or contacts weren't added, sent, or deleted.

4. Escalations

There shouldn't be a need to escalate for this state as it is mostly informational. However, if a customer is repeatedly having issues with this and insists on escalating, go to KB 817557 Guidelines: Advanced Capability Advocate engagement for valid escalations.
