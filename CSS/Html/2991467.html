1. What is personal data?

Any information relating to an identified or identifiable individual. This includes authenticated and unauthenticated data, linked or linkable to a PUID, Device ID, ANID, or any similar ID that singles out a user.

An identifiable individual can be directly or indirectly identified by:

  1. A name, an identification number, location data, or online identifier.
  2. One or more factors specific to the physical, psychological, genetic, mental, economic, cultural, or social identity of that individual.

To ensure correct handling of personal data It's important that you understand Microsoft's privacy and data protection requirements and incorporate strong privacy practices in your day-to-day work.

For full details on handling personal data see KB 3064766 Procedure: Privacy: Handling Microsoft customer personal data.

2. Social engineering

2.1 What is social engineering?

Social engineering refers to psychologically manipulating people into performing actions or providing confidential information. This could be done through tactics such as phishing, malware/spyware, or account hacking.

Social engineering differs from a traditional "con" in that it is often one of many steps in a more complex fraud scheme, sometimes involving phishing and identify theft.

  • An attacker will persistently probe Microsoft Support to obtain enough information to change a customer’s Microsoft account password, allowing the attacker to take over the customer’s account.
  • This is known as account hacking and results in the customer no longer having access to their account.
  • When the customer tries to log in to the account using their password, they can't.

Protecting customers from social engineering
To significantly reduce the chances of falling victim to a social engineering scam, it is imperative to follow these practices:

  • Always follow Microsoft's privacy and security policies.
  • Never give hints to customers about the information in their session/account. For example, don't tell a customer that their guesses are close or give them the first letter to words.
  • Never divulge information about the support tools you are using; for example, don't divulge the names of tools or what you can use for search criteria or what you can or can't do or see on the tool screens.
  • Never divulge information to a person who calls claiming to be a Microsoft representative stating, for example, that a tool is undergoing maintenance, even if this person provides an email address or phone number of a real Microsoft employee.

2.2 Impacts of social engineering on Microsoft

  • Negatively affects Microsoft's reputation with customers and, in some cases, can result in unwanted media attention.
  • Lowers customer satisfaction and erodes customer trust in Microsoft's data-protection practices.
  • Might hurt Microsoft's ability to retain customers or obtain new business.
  • Microsoft Support loses time and money resolving customer issues resulting from social engineering.

If customers trust the way Microsoft protects their personal information, they're more likely to choose us over a competitor.

It is essential that you incorporate these privacy policies into your day-to-day work, not only to protect customer information but to ensure continued customer satisfaction and trust in Microsoft.

Even if your role is not customer-facing, understanding these principles helps Microsoft foster a culture of privacy throughout the services organization.

2.3 Phishing

What is phishing?

Attempts to acquire general or sensitive information associated with specific people or businesses is referred to as phishing. Phishing attempts commonly occur via phone, email, or deceptive websites, allowing for the collection of sensitive personal data related to customer accounts, business practices, and products. This may be a customer providing their personal data to a third party posing as Microsoft or a caller trying to obtain information from support on someone else’s account.

Information obtained through phishing may be used to:

  • Impersonate an account owner to obtain additional information (for example: account usage, billing and payment instrument (PI) information, or personal data).
  • Aid in the theft of a customer’s Microsoft account/gamertag.
  • Gain understanding of internal tools, policies, or business jargon (allowing for advocate impersonation).
  • Acquire information regarding new products and services.

While supporting Microsoft customers, you may encounter traditional phishing attempts or scenarios such as:

  • Individual phishing for information (accounts or process information).
  • Individual phishing for excessive refunds or credits.
  • Customer who reports a website they believe is being used in phishing or selling fraudulently obtained tokens, products, or Microsoft product keys.
  • Customer has experienced other users attempting to extort funds or content in exchange for restoring the Xbox Live service.
  • Customer has personal knowledge about attackers or attacker groups claiming responsibility for Xbox Live outages.
  • Customer reports being contacted by a business claiming to be Microsoft or contacting them on the behalf of Microsoft.

3. Identity theft

3.1 What is identity theft?

‎Identity theft is the deliberate use of someone else's identity, usually as a method to gain a financial advantage or obtain credit and other benefits in the other person's name, and sometimes to the other person's disadvantage or loss. The person whose identity has been assumed may suffer adverse consequences if they are held responsible for the perpetrator's actions. Identity theft occurs when someone uses another's personally identifying information, like their name, identifying number, or credit card number, without their permission, to commit fraud or other crimes.

Identifying phishing attempts:

  • Customer asking for personal data including email address, phone number, or console ID before authenticating their account.
  • Asking about Microsoft internal process and tools such as our authentication process or refund exception policies, or asking to access recent account activities.

3.2 Identifying fraud incidents

A fraud incident is any deliberate deception to obtain products or services to which one is not entitled or has no claim to receive.

During the account review, look for repeat contacts over a short period, signs of service abuse, and other unusual circumstances. Always follow documented policy whenever assisting a customer, even if fraud is suspected.

Examples include:

  • Person claiming to be a member of an internal support team or law enforcement agency asking specific questions about a customer account, policy, products, services, tools, or internal information.
  • Excessive requests for refunds or subscription cancellations.
  • Multiple device or console exchanges associated with a single account.
  • Numerous regular, special edition, or multiregional consoles or devices registered to one account.
  • Feedback within the notes indicating fraud, phishing, or questionable activity.
  • Frequent contact to support for a variety of issues or the exact same issue (for example: extensive history of refund requests, subscription cancellations, or token issues).
  • Customer contacts support for an explicit issue but focuses the conversation on account specific or unrelated topics before abruptly disconnecting.
  • Attacks or extortion attempts against Microsoft or Xbox Live services, such as presenting information about another user's attempts to extort funds or content in exchange for restoring a Microsoft service.

4. Steps to take prior to reporting an incident

Review account activity to determine if the customer is possibly engaging in fraud.

To improve Microsoft’s ability to identify and hold fraudulent parties accountable:

  • Clearly communicate when a customer’s request is denied.
  • Document within the service request when a customer' request is denied and summarize the reasons why it was denied.
  • Determine if there is a history of policy abuse by reviewing previous service requests and escalate if the customer has already received a denial message for the same or similar requests.
  • If there are no previous service requests on the account, search for the customer by using their phone number to see if there are related accounts. Report if you find multiple accounts associated with the same phone number showing similar behavior.

Do not report if:

The account does not show a history of refund/credit, promotion, subscription, or hardware replacement abuse:

  • There are no previous SRs on the account in question and no secondary accounts are located when searching with the customer’s phone number (for example, there is no history of possible fraud or abuse linked with the account)
  • A denial message has not been previously delivered and documented by another support advocate.

Reporting process

To report fraudulent customer account activity in MST see KB 4463303 TUG: MST: Reporting fraud or phishing
